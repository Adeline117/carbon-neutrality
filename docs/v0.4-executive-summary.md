# Executive Summary — On-Chain Carbon Credit Quality Rating (v0.4)

*Two-page briefing for prospective expert reviewers. For the full methodology see `docs/workshop-paper.md`; for the scoring mechanism decision see `docs/methodology-gate-v0.4.md`.*

## The problem

When carbon credits are tokenized and pooled into fungible on-chain instruments, the pool's price stops reflecting the quality of individual credits. Credit holders rationally deposit their lowest-quality eligible credits (same pool price, easier liquidity) and withhold their best ones (higher OTC prices). The pool degrades toward adverse selection — the classic "market for lemons" (Akerlof 1970), formally modeled for the voluntary carbon market by Manshadi, Monachou, and Morgenstern (2025).

**Toucan Protocol's BCT (Base Carbon Tonne) pool is the canonical example.** BCT's minimal eligibility (any Verra VCU, vintage ≥ 2008, non-excluded methodology) produced a pool dominated by pre-2018 REDD+, HFC-23 destruction, and old wind credits. Empirical assessments have since confirmed that fewer than 16% of voluntary carbon credits represent real emission reductions (Nature Communications 2024), and that 87% of offsets bought by major companies carry high risk of no climate impact (Trencher et al. 2024). BCT pooled exactly the credits the market learned to distrust.

Off-chain rating agencies (Sylvera, BeZero, Calyx Global, MSCI) have developed project-level quality ratings, but they are:

- **Opaque** — detailed scoring criteria are not public
- **Inconsistent** — a 2023 Carbon Market Watch study found significant inter-agency disagreement (the same Amazon REDD+ project received high marks from Sylvera but low from Calyx and BeZero)
- **Off-chain** — not natively readable by DeFi protocols that would gate pool deposits

We propose a **transparent, multi-dimensional, open-source quality rating framework** designed to run on-chain, so that quality differentiation can be enforced by smart contracts rather than trust.

## The framework in one paragraph

Each carbon credit receives seven per-dimension scores in [0, 100]: **removal type** (Oxford hierarchy — removal > avoidance), **additionality**, **permanence**, **MRV grade**, **vintage year**, **co-benefits**, **registry/methodology**. A weighted linear composite maps to one of six grades: **AAA → AA → A → BBB → BB → B**. Six disqualifier flags (double counting, failed verification, sanctioned registry, no third-party verification, human rights concerns, community harm) cap the final grade regardless of composite. The full rubric is machine-readable JSON (`data/scoring-rubrics/`), the composite math is a Python scorer and a Solidity contract that produce identical results to the basis point, and the contract enforces quality-gated deposit rules (`contracts/QualityGatedPool.sol`).

## What changed in v0.4 — and why it matters

v0.3 surfaced a problem during pilot scoring: **no credit reached AAA**. Climeworks Orca, the top-scoring credit, topped out at 86.8 because co-benefits (10% weight) systematically penalized industrial Direct Air Capture projects that have no SDG narrative. This inverted the Oxford Principles hierarchy the framework is supposed to encode.

We stress-tested four candidate fixes (`docs/methodology-gate-v0.4.md`) and picked the **safeguards-gate mechanism**:

- **Co-benefits is no longer scored in the composite.** Its 10% weight is redistributed to the technical dimensions (removal_type 0.25, mrv_grade 0.20, permanence 0.175).
- **Co-benefits becomes a harm filter instead.** Assessors still attest a 0-100 co-benefits score for informational use, but a score in the 0-9 "None / negative externalities" band triggers a new `communityHarm` disqualifier that caps the grade at BBB.

The rationale draws directly on Berg et al. (2025), who showed that buyers of voluntary carbon credits already pay a 2× premium for co-benefit narratives *regardless of underlying integrity*. Rewarding co-benefits in a quality rating reinforces exactly the mispricing a quality rating should correct. The safeguards-gate preserves the protective function of co-benefits (bad community impacts still cap the grade) without letting narratives inflate the composite.

Three alternatives were rejected during the gate process: a +5 removal bonus (triple-counts durability, left Toucan's one decent BCT credit fragile at 75.3 AA by 0.3 points); a multiplicative 1.05 premium (same triple-count problem plus nonlinear math unfriendly to Solidity); and a geometric-mean technical core (required cube roots on-chain and failed to get Orca to AAA).

## What the pilot shows

**25 illustrative credits + 4 synthetic disqualifier stress tests + 14 real tokenized on-chain credits.**

1. **Oxford hierarchy restored at the top.** Under v0.4, Climeworks Orca, Heirloom DAC, and Charm Industrial bio-oil all reach AAA. Under v0.3 all three topped out at AA.
2. **Toucan BCT is measurably BB.** Scored as an actual pool (not as individual credits inside), BCT lands at 31.1 — BB, but only 1.1 points above the B boundary. Any modest downward rescoring pushes the entire BCT pool into the lowest grade tier. This is a stronger empirical statement than commercial rating agencies have been able to make about BCT.
3. **Moss MCO2 is even more fragile.** At 30.5 BB (0.5 above B) — driven by Amazon REDD+ concentration, consistent with West et al. (Science, 2023) findings on REDD+ baseline inflation.
4. **The AA middle is empty in the tokenized market.** Of the 14 real tokenized credits scored under v0.4, no nature-based credit reached AA. The only AA is Puro's biochar average (80.4). High-quality nature-based credits have not migrated to on-chain form — a market-structure finding the paper flags as an open question.
5. **The disqualifier lattice works.** Three synthetic stress tests (C026-C028) demonstrate that double counting, sanctioned registry, and no-third-party flags correctly cap otherwise AA-level credits at B, BB, and BBB respectively. A fourth (C029) tests the new v0.4 communityHarm gate and confirms it caps at BBB. Flowcarbon — the real-world Celsius-adjacent nature-based token that failed to launch — correctly caps at B via `failed_verification` on the tokenized pilot.

## What we need from you

We are seeking feedback from experts in carbon markets, registry operations, DeFi protocol design, and climate science. **Specifically, we are not asking for a rubber-stamp** on the v0.4 weights or the safeguards-gate mechanism. We are asking you to react to the decision menu we considered, challenge the tradeoffs, and surface things we missed.

A structured weight-calibration questionnaire is at `docs/v0.5-weight-calibration-questionnaire.md`. Questions we are most interested in:

1. **Is the safeguards-gate the right mechanism?** Or would you argue for keeping co-benefits as a scored dimension, reducing it further, or introducing a geometric-mean technical core (which we rejected for on-chain reasons but which may be defensible off-chain)?
2. **Are the weights defensible?** We shifted 10 percentage points from co-benefits into technical dimensions and 2.5 points out of registry/methodology into removal_type. How would you reallocate?
3. **Are the disqualifier caps calibrated correctly?** Human rights caps at B, community harm caps at BBB. Is that the right gradient, or should any community-impact concern cap at B?
4. **Should removal_type and permanence be merged into a single "durability" dimension?** Finding 4 of the v0.3 pilot raised concerns about collinearity; the v0.4 sensitivity analysis showed permanence doing independent work (5 of 29 leave-one-out flips). We are inclined to keep them separate but would defer to expert opinion.
5. **Are there credits in our illustrative or tokenized pilots where our per-dimension scores are clearly wrong?** We would rather hear this early than ship a paper that misrates a named project.
6. **Is the off-chain ≡ on-chain invariant the right design constraint?** We committed to preserving linear composition so the Python scorer and Solidity contract produce identical results. This rules out some mechanisms (geomean, multiplicative premiums with fixed-point math). A reviewer who believes on-chain is the wrong venue would want us to drop this constraint.
