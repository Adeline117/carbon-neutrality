# Cover Letter -- Environmental Research Letters

Dear Editors,

We submit for your consideration the manuscript "An open, distributional quality framework for voluntary carbon credits: validation against regulatory thresholds and commercial ratings" as a Letter to Environmental Research Letters. This work presents the first open, reproducible quality framework for voluntary carbon credits that produces distributional grade posteriors -- probability distributions over letter grades rather than point estimates -- enabling transparent uncertainty quantification at the individual credit level. The framework scores credits across seven dimensions with published weights and machine-readable rubrics, filling a structural gap in carbon market infrastructure where proprietary ratings dominate and no open benchmark exists.

This manuscript is directly relevant to your readership because it addresses a measurable crisis in environmental market integrity. Commercial carbon credit rating agencies -- the entities on which billion-dollar corporate offset portfolios depend -- agree with each other at a mean Spearman correlation of +0.009 on the same projects. BeZero Carbon and Calyx Global are anti-correlated at rho = -0.664 on a six-project REDD+ overlap sample: they systematically disagree on which projects are higher and lower quality. Our framework's mean correlation with these agencies (+0.343) exceeds their mutual agreement, and it is the first system to publish P(grade) posteriors that quantify how close a credit sits to a grade boundary. This distributional transparency is absent from all existing commercial and regulatory quality systems.

We report three principal findings. First, the framework independently recovers the ICVCM Core Carbon Principles quality threshold with a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits (Cohen's d = 1.80, common language effect size = 91.4%, n = 318), converging on Calyx Global's independently published measurement without training on CCP labels. Second, Spearman rank correlation with BeZero Carbon reaches +0.901 (95% CI [+0.783, +0.959], p < 0.0001) across 27 projects spanning 12 credit types, with subgroup correlations of +0.973 for carbon dioxide removal and +0.802 for avoidance credits. Third, a quality atlas across 34 market segments quantifies adverse selection severity via the Lemons Index, which ranges from 0.076 (direct air capture) to 0.759 (renewable energy), establishing the empirical landscape of credit quality heterogeneity.

The timing of this contribution aligns with three concurrent regulatory developments. Singapore's National Environment Agency in November 2025 became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit quality ratings for compliance-market eligibility, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as official raters -- yet these agencies cannot agree on the quality of the same projects. The ICVCM's CCP rollout has now approved 36 methodologies covering approximately 101 million credits, creating urgent demand for continuous quality differentiation above the binary CCP threshold. ICAO's CORSIA Phase 2 compliance period (2027--2029) projects demand of 146--236 million emissions units, and programme-level approval alone has proven insufficient to ensure credit-level quality. An open benchmark framework against which these regulatory and commercial systems can be independently evaluated is timely and necessary.

We confirm that all authors have approved the manuscript, that the work has not been published previously nor is under consideration elsewhere, and that there are no conflicts of interest. All data, code, and scoring rubrics are available at https://github.com/Adeline117/carbon-neutrality under an MIT licence. This manuscript is a companion to an empirical paper on adverse selection in tokenized carbon credit pools (submitted to Nature Communications) and a systems paper on composable on-chain quality infrastructure (submitted to WWW 2027); each is self-contained and makes independent contributions.
