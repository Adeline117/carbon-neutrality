# Discussion and Abstract --- Nature Communications Draft

**Paper**: "Anatomy of a market failure: on-chain evidence reveals renewable energy credits, not REDD+, drove the collapse of tokenized carbon markets"

---

## Discussion

### The REDD+ narrative was wrong

The most consequential finding in this study is compositional: Toucan BCT, the largest and most scrutinised tokenized carbon credit pool, was 69.1% renewable energy credits and 4.2% REDD+. The prevailing academic and industry narrative --- that BCT collapsed because of low-quality REDD+ projects with contested baselines --- is factually incorrect with respect to the pool's actual on-chain composition. This matters because the narrative has shaped policy: pool designers have focused on excluding nature-based credits, regulators have prioritised REDD+ oversight, and the broader VCM discourse has treated REDD+ as the primary quality problem. Our data show that the primary quality problem was elsewhere entirely.

The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial --- high-profile investigations of Kariba, Cordillera Azul, and Amazon basin projects generated extensive media coverage and academic scrutiny^1,2,3^ --- while the quality problems of CDM-era renewable energy credits are structurally invisible. A Chinese wind farm registered in 2010 does not generate controversy: the wind farm exists, the electricity is real, the monitoring data are accurate. The quality failure is not in the project's execution but in its additionality: the wind farm would have been built regardless of carbon revenue, because grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010. This form of quality failure --- a project that delivers real outputs but no additional emission reductions --- is harder to detect, less newsworthy, and more prevalent than the baseline manipulation problems that dominate the REDD+ discourse.

The implication for policy is direct: quality infrastructure must address the additionality dimension with the same rigour currently applied to baseline accuracy. The ICVCM's decision not to grant CCP eligibility to grid-connected renewable energy credits is consistent with our findings, but the VCM still contains billions of tonnes of legacy renewable energy credits that can be deposited into any pool that does not actively exclude them. BCT's composition demonstrates what happens when they are not excluded.

### Renewable energy credits are the hidden quality crisis

Our 34-segment quality atlas positions grid-connected renewables at the bottom of the quality spectrum (PQD = 0.759), below even the contested REDD+ categories (PQD 0.69--0.72). This finding has not been previously documented at the credit level, though it is consistent with aggregate assessments: Calel et al.^4^ estimated that fewer than 16% of credits represent real emission reductions, and the bulk of non-additional credits in their sample were avoidance-based, including renewables. Trencher et al.^5^ found that 87% of corporate offset purchases carry high risk of non-additionality, with renewable energy credits accounting for a substantial share.

The quality atlas reveals that the VCM's quality problem is not uniform: it is concentrated in specific segments (renewables, industrial gas destruction, pre-2013 avoidance credits) while other segments (engineered CDR, biochar, recent cookstoves with digital MRV) exhibit quality profiles that could support functional markets. The 10-fold PQD range from 0.076 to 0.759 means that treating the VCM as a single market with a single quality level is misleading. There are multiple quality markets operating under a single label.

### Low-type pooling equilibrium: recovery requires quality infrastructure

BCT's PQD of 0.679 with 89.8% of tonnage at BB or B represents a low-type pooling equilibrium consistent with Gresham's law applied to uniform-price pools: low-quality credits drive out high-quality ones when the market fails to differentiate. The price collapse from $7 to $0.50 confirms that the market ultimately priced BCT at the reservation value of its dominant supply --- CDM-era renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality. BCT continued to trade (ruling out Akerlof's strict "no trade" endstate), but at a price that could only attract the lowest-quality credits, producing the pooling equilibrium we observe.

### Gresham dynamics and the limits of causal identification

The base-rate comparison (1.87$\times$ over-selection, $p$ < 10$^{-187}$) is the paper's strongest test. It requires no external control pool, relying only on BCT's composition versus the VCS-eligible universe. The over-selection is robust to wallet-level clustering: a wallet-clustered permutation test ($p$ <0.0001, 10,000 iterations) and an HHI-adjusted binomial ($n_{\text{eff}}$ = 83.5, $p$ = 2.9 $\times$ 10$^{-15}$) confirm that the selection result is not an artefact of concentrated depositors. The bootstrap selection coefficient of 0.522 [0.496, 0.547] (BCa 95% CI) quantifies the magnitude of over-selection at the wallet level.

The redemption-side analysis provides the complementary margin of the Gresham dynamic. High-quality credits were selectively redeemed out of BCT: REDD+ at 99.8%, industrial gas at 100%, IFM at 93%, and ARR at 91.3%, while renewable energy credits --- the dominant low-quality supply --- were redeemed at only 3.6%. The tonnage-weighted mean quality of redeemed credits (38.7) exceeded that of deposited credits (31.7) by 7 points. The net effect was to increase BCT's renewable share from 69.1% (deposit-side) to 78.2% (net of redemptions), amplifying the quality collapse. This is Gresham's law documented on both the entry and exit margins of a single market.

Critically, redemption timing exhibits a temporal ordering by quality: higher-quality credits were redeemed earlier ($\rho$ = -0.095, $p$ = 2.6e-71), consistent with informed actors extracting undervalued credits before the pool's quality floor became fully priced. The depositor and redeemer populations are nearly disjoint --- only 1.4% of the 28897 unique redeemer wallets also appear as depositors among the 509 deposit wallets --- ruling out the alternative explanation that the same actors gamed both the deposit and redemption margins. The dual-margin selection pattern thus reflects two distinct populations responding independently to BCT's mispricing of quality: depositors exploiting overvaluation of low-quality credits, and redeemers exploiting undervaluation of high-quality ones.

The Granger causality results provide the mechanism link that connects composition to price. At weekly frequency ($n$ = 55), quality composition Granger-causes price ($F$ = 6.32, $p$ = 0.004) and price Granger-causes quality composition ($F$ = 16.08, $p$ <10^-5). The asymmetry in $F$-statistics indicates that the price $\rightarrow$ quality channel is stronger: price declines attracted lower-quality deposits, which further depressed price, producing a self-reinforcing feedback loop. This bidirectional causality was absent in rolling 30-day quality metrics, confirming that the feedback operates through accumulated pool composition rather than marginal deposit quality.

A potential objection to the Gresham framing is that Gresham's law historically requires a legal tender mandate --- a compulsion to accept bad money at par with good. We argue that BCT's uniform pricing mechanism functions as the de facto mandate within the pool: all credits deposited into BCT receive the same BCT token regardless of quality, and the pool price applies uniformly to all deposited credits. The redemption evidence addresses this objection directly: credits that had higher value elsewhere (REDD+ and nature-based credits with active retirement demand) were selectively redeemed out of BCT and presumably retired or sold at their quality-differentiated price on non-tokenized markets. BCT's uniform pricing thus created exactly the conditions Gresham described --- undervalued good credits exited, overvalued bad credits remained.

We acknowledge key identification limits. The BCT--NCT comparison cannot support causal inference (88.6% token overlap, 19 shared wallets, NCT designed post-BCT; cluster-robust $p$ = 0.24). The temporal decline ($\rho$ = -0.24) is primarily a vintage-composition effect (vintage-free $\rho$ = +0.24). The compositional shift (renewable share Q1 67% $\rightarrow$ Q4 99.5%) is descriptively clear but causally ambiguous --- Gresham exit, differential supply, or both. What this study establishes is the *composition*, *selection coefficient*, and *dual-margin selection pattern* of quality failure. The Gresham framing --- uniform pricing over observable quality heterogeneity drives out high-value goods --- is more appropriate than Akerlof (which requires asymmetric information not present here, since Verra metadata was public).

### Recovery requires quality infrastructure

The significance of reaching a low-type pooling equilibrium is that recovery requires a discontinuous intervention. Once a pool is dominated by low-quality supply, incremental improvements in average quality are insufficient: buyers have exited, trust has been destroyed, and the market-clearing price is too low to attract high-quality supply. Recovery requires the introduction of a credible quality differentiation mechanism that enables the market to price quality at the point of transaction.

In the carbon market context, quality gating serves as this mechanism. Pools that restrict deposits by credit type or allowlist exhibit descriptively better quality outcomes: BCT (no gate, PQD 0.679), NCT (type filter, PQD 0.601), CHAR (allowlist, PQD 0.221). We note that this gradient is observational, and the BCT--NCT comparison is confounded by extensive token overlap (88.6%) and endogenous pool design. Nevertheless, our counterfactual simulations show that a continuous quality gate at the BBB threshold would have reduced BCT's PQD from 0.679 to 0.405, and a gate at BB would have achieved 0.547. The Klima 2.0 kVCM inventory on Base, which applies curated selection, achieves a PQD of 0.519 without explicit quality scoring --- demonstrating that even informal curation shifts the quality distribution toward higher quality.

The redemption analysis reveals an important design implication: quality gating must operate on both the deposit and redemption margins. BCT's quality degraded not only because low-quality credits were preferentially deposited but also because high-quality credits were preferentially redeemed. A deposit-side quality gate alone would prevent the inflow of low-quality credits but would not prevent the selective outflow of high-quality ones. Effective pool design must either (i) apply quality-differentiated pricing at redemption (charging a premium to redeem high-quality credits, thereby discouraging selective withdrawal) or (ii) maintain quality floors on the net pool composition by restricting redemptions that would push the pool below a quality threshold.

The practical implication is that market recovery requires not just better credits but better quality infrastructure: systems that make quality observable, comparable, and enforceable at the point of transaction. Singapore's 2025 mandate for commercial carbon credit ratings^6^ represents the first sovereign step in this direction; the on-chain implementation described in our companion paper^7^ provides the technical mechanism for enforcement.

### Agency disagreement and the need for open quality infrastructure

The BeZero--Calyx anti-correlation of $\rho$ = -0.664 on the 6-project REDD+ overlap sample^8^ means that two major carbon credit rating agencies produce rankings that are worse than random on the same projects. The mean inter-agency Spearman of +0.009 across all three agency pairs is indistinguishable from zero. This disagreement is concentrated in credit categories (REDD+) where counterfactual baselines are inherently uncertain; on cross-methodology datasets where quality signals are clearer, our framework converges with BeZero at $\rho$ = +0.901 ($n$ = 27).

The combination of composition misattribution, quality collapse, and agency disagreement points to a structural diagnosis: the VCM lacks transparent, reproducible quality infrastructure. Our framework addresses this gap through openness (machine-readable rubrics, open-source scoring engine, published dataset), continuous quality differentiation (six-tier grades, continuous PQD), and composability (on-chain `meetsGrade()` interface enabling quality gates at the smart contract level, detailed in our companion paper^7^).

### Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the quality scores are author-derived. Although validated against CCP labels ($d$ = 1.8), commercial ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901, $n$ = 27), and an LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.6), the rubric reflects a single group's judgment. The inter-rater study uses three Claude-family models that share training data, potentially producing correlated biases; multi-provider replication is needed. The PQD metric embeds normative assumptions (Oxford hierarchy, specific weights); all rubrics are published for re-weighting.

Second, the cross-pool comparison (BCT, NCT, CHAR) is observational. BCT and NCT share 88.6% of tokens and 19 depositor wallets; cluster-robust inference renders the naive correlation difference non-significant ($p$ = 0.24 vs. naive $p$ = 2.3 $\times$ 10$^{-7}$). No independent control pool operated during BCT's lifetime; causal identification of pool-mechanism effects remains an open question.

Third, the temporal decline ($\rho$ = -0.24) is driven by the vintage-year component of the composite. A vintage-free robustness check reverses the sign ($\rho$ = +0.24), indicating vintage-composition drift rather than within-vintage quality deterioration. We cannot distinguish strategic dumping from supply exhaustion.

Fourth, the BeZero BCT validation covers only 7 projects ($p$ = 0.073, not significant at $\alpha$ = 0.05). BCT composition relies on Verra registry metadata without independent verification, though the 69.1% renewable dominance is robust to misclassification.

Fifth, enumerating all TCO2 tokens created by Toucan's factory contract on Polygon reveals that 369 unique tokens were bridged before 2023, of which 345 (93.5%) were deposited into BCT. Only 24 bridged tokens never entered the pool. This near-complete pass-through (93.5%) means that the 1.87$\times$ renewable over-selection documented in Section 2.2 is primarily a bridge-level phenomenon: Toucan's permissionless bridge disproportionately attracted renewable energy credits from Verra, and BCT absorbed the bridged universe almost in its entirety. Pool-level deposit selection --- depositors strategically choosing renewables from a diverse bridged set --- played a minimal role on the entry margin. The Gresham dynamic is therefore asymmetric: architectural on entry (the bridge channelled low-quality supply) and strategic on exit (distinct, concentrated actors selectively redeemed high-quality credits out of the uniform-price pool).

## Abstract

The collapse of Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- from $7 to $0.50 --- is widely attributed to low-quality REDD+ credits. We analyse all 1187 on-chain deposit transactions (168 VCS projects, 22 million tonnes) on Polygon and find this narrative is wrong. BCT was 69.1% renewable energy credits and just 4.2% REDD+. This represents 1.87$\times$ over-selection relative to the Verra VCS base rate of 37% (wallet-clustered permutation $p$ <0.0001; robust across base-rate assumptions up to 55%). Using a quality framework validated against CCP labels ($d$ = 1.8) and BeZero ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901, $n$ = 27), we compute BCT's Pool Quality Deficit at 0.679 --- a low-type pooling equilibrium with 60.7% at grade B. We connect composition to price through a bidirectional feedback loop: quality composition Granger-causes price movements ($p$ = 0.004) and price declines attract lower-quality deposits ($p$ <10^-5). Redemption-side analysis reveals the complementary mechanism: high-quality credits (REDD+ 99.8%, IFM 93%) were selectively redeemed out while renewables (3.6% redeemed) accumulated, with depositor and redeemer populations nearly disjoint (1.4% overlap) --- the Gresham dynamic documented on both margins by independent actors. A 34-segment quality atlas reveals 10-fold quality variation across the VCM (0.076 for direct air capture to 0.759 for renewables). Quality-gated pools show descriptively better outcomes (CHAR PQD = 0.221), though cross-pool comparisons are observational. These findings demonstrate that the prevailing narrative about what destroyed the first major tokenized carbon market was factually wrong, and that transparent quality infrastructure is the prerequisite for market recovery.
