**Paper**: "Adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets: who profited from the first pool collapse"

## Discussion

The informal narrative around BCT's collapse^16,17,18^ attributes it to REDD+ credits with contested baselines --- a plausible frame given the broader academic evidence of REDD+ overcrediting^2,4^ but one never verified against BCT's recorded composition. Our forensic reconstruction reveals a different picture: the pool was {{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% renewable energy credits --- CDM-era projects with near-zero additionality --- while a small number of type-specialist accounts extracted the credits that off-chain markets still valued. The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial, while the additionality failure of CDM-era renewables is structurally invisible: the wind farms exist, the electricity is real, but grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010.

Our contribution is distinct from recent related work. Manshadi et al.^5^ model adverse selection in the VCM theoretically; we provide the first credit-level empirical test, showing that BCT's lack of quality certification produced outcomes consistent with the lemons dynamics their model predicts, albeit through observable rather than private quality heterogeneity. Probst et al.^2^ and Calel et al.^46^ quantify overcrediting at the project level; we show what happens when those overcredited projects enter a pooled market --- how quality heterogeneity translates into participant-level extraction and \$4--8 million in realised profits.

A natural objection is that BCT's collapse reflects cryptocurrency pathologies rather than a structural carbon market problem. We argue the opposite. The mechanism we document is a variant of Akerlof's (1970) lemons problem: classical adverse selection assumes private information, but BCT provides empirical evidence that uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets can produce the same Gresham dynamic even when quality is fully observable, suggesting that transparency alone is insufficient to prevent quality collapse without quality-differentiated pricing. This design-enabled adverse selection operates identically whether the ledger is a blockchain, a centralised exchange, or a paper registry. Three features distinguish this from a generic crypto collapse: (i) the underlying assets are real-world carbon credits with Verra registry entries; (ii) quality heterogeneity is determined by project-level additionality and permanence, not market sentiment; and (iii) the same Gresham dynamic operates in traditional carbon registries^29,43^. The policy implication --- that quality-differentiated pricing is necessary wherever heterogeneous carbon credits are pooled --- applies equally to registry-based markets and Article 6.4.

The 98.8% stranding rate for B-grade credits is the starkest outcome: nearly 10 million tonnes of nominally valid offsets are functionally worthless. We estimate the welfare gap at \$100--250 million (Monte Carlo median \${{welfare.welfare_gap_median_usd_millions}}M, 90% CI \$68M--\$252M). Paradoxically, BCT inadvertently removed 9.6 million tonnes of low-quality credits from active circulation --- a permanent illiquidity event no registry-level intervention has achieved. The pool's failure as a market may have been an inadvertent success as a quality filter.

BCT is a single pool, but three features make its mechanism generalizable. First, the underlying economic logic --- Gresham's Law under uniform pricing --- is not specific to blockchain or to carbon; it applies wherever heterogeneous assets are pooled at a single price, including registry-based VCM transactions where fungible credits of varying quality clear against undifferentiated compliance obligations. Second, the Article 6.4 mechanism under the Paris Agreement is designing exactly such pooled crediting systems at sovereign scale; BCT provides a fully observable test case of what happens when quality differentiation is absent^42,43^. Third, the tokenized real-world asset sector (\$10 billion and growing) is replicating the pool-based architecture across commodities, bonds, and receivables --- BCT is the first pool to complete a full quality-collapse cycle with auditable transaction data.

Independent cross-domain evidence supports the generality of the pattern. First, Curve Finance's staked-ETH/ETH liquidity pool (see Methods) shifted by $\sim$\$762M toward the lower-quality asset during the May 2022 crash, with composition shifts 40$\times$ baseline. Second, an analysis of 20 NFTX vaults --- uniform-price pools of non-fungible tokens on Ethereum, an entirely different asset class --- reveals the same dual-margin structure: 12 of 20 vaults show net redemption outflow (selective extraction of higher-value items), and the largest vault (MILADY) exhibits a minter--redeemer overlap rate of 1.3%, near-identical to BCT's 1.4% (see Supplementary Methods). The BCT case is chronic quality degradation; the Curve case is acute shock response; the NFTX case is steady-state cherry-picking across heterogeneous collectibles. Three independent asset classes (carbon credits, staking derivatives, NFTs) exhibiting the same pattern under uniform pricing strengthens the claim that the mechanism is general, though all three operate on blockchain infrastructure and further evidence from traditional markets would be valuable.

Recovery from a low-type equilibrium requires discontinuous intervention. A quality-volume frontier sweep reveals that even a minimal quality floor (composite $\geq$29) would have excluded the worst half of BCT's tonnage. Critically, gating must operate on both margins: BCT's dual-margin forensics show that exit-side extraction was at least as damaging as entry-side quality loading. Our findings provide direct empirical support for the ICVCM's Core Carbon Principles framework^8^: without quality differentiation, pooled markets can systematically allocate low-quality residuals to passive participants. The emerging ecosystem --- Singapore's carbon credit ratings mandate^15^, blockchain-based credibility indices^20^, and quality-gating protocols^10^ --- must operate on both entry and exit margins.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. The quality scores are author-derived, but core findings (composition, base-rate selection, type-level redemption differentials, within-token cross-pool comparison, compositional DiD) rely on transaction data and Verra credit-type classifications, not on our scoring framework. The framework is validated against CCP labels ($d$ = {{quality.ccp_cohens_d}}), commercial ratings ($\rho$ = +{{quality.bezero_rho}}, $n$ = 27), and an LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.6), with removal-type sensitivity and multi-provider replication checks (see Supplementary Methods). The cross-pool comparison is observational; causal identification of pool-mechanism effects remains open. The temporal decline ($\rho$ = $-$0.24) is a vintage-composition effect (vintage-free $\rho$ = +0.24). The bridge decomposition ({{bridge_decomposition.bct_coverage_pct}}% pass-through; 99.6% by tonnage) establishes that entry-side selection is primarily architectural rather than strategic --- the design-enabled adverse selection operated through which credit types were attracted to tokenization in the first place (bridge-level), not through depositors cherry-picking within the bridged universe (pool-level). This is consistent with the design-enabled framing: the architecture created the conditions for quality collapse without requiring deliberate manipulation at any single stage.

## Abstract

Carbon markets are central to global climate policy, yet the quality of carbon credits varies widely and mechanisms for preventing low-quality credits from dominating trade remain poorly understood. When credits of different quality are sold at a single price, economic theory predicts that low-quality assets will drive out high-quality ones, but empirical evidence for this dynamic in carbon markets has been lacking. Here, we show that the collapse of the largest pooled carbon credit market (22 million tonnes) was driven by renewable energy credits with near-zero climate benefit ({{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% of pool content), not forest-based credits ({{composition.redd_pct_tonnes}}%) as widely claimed. Analysing all {{composition.n_deposits}} deposits, {{redemption.n_transfer_events}} withdrawals, and 28,897 participant accounts, we find that two nearly separate populations (1.4% overlap) operated on opposite sides of the market: sellers deposited credits indiscriminately, while a small group of informed buyers selectively extracted the credits that retained value elsewhere. Five accounts extracted 1.55 million tonnes; 9.6 million tonnes of low-quality credits remain permanently stranded. Comparison with a quality-screened pool confirms that market design, not credit type, determined asset fate, demonstrating that uniform pricing of heterogeneous carbon credits produces systematic quality collapse even when quality differences are publicly observable.
