# Blockchain transparency without quality signals accelerates adverse selection in carbon markets: depositor-level evidence from tokenized credit pools

## 1. Introduction

Blockchain technology entered the voluntary carbon market (VCM) with a specific promise: radical transparency would solve the trust deficit that had plagued carbon offsetting for decades. Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool, launched on Polygon in October 2021 and amplified by KlimaDAO's treasury accumulation, became the flagship of this vision. Every deposit, every redemption, every retirement was recorded on an immutable public ledger. By November 2021, BCT held millions of tonnes of tokenized carbon credits and traded above $8 per tonne. The transparency narrative appeared vindicated.

Within eighteen months, the experiment had collapsed. BCT's price fell below $1, its inventory was dominated by credits that independent assessments would subsequently identify as low-integrity --- decade-old industrial gas destruction credits, pre-2013 renewable energy certificates, and poorly verified REDD+ projects with contested baselines^1,2^. Verra, the largest carbon credit registry, issued formal restrictions on unauthorized tokenization^3^. The market confidence that transparency was supposed to build had evaporated.

The paradox is precise: BCT made every transaction visible while leaving credit quality invisible. A 2009 HFC-23 destruction credit and a 2022 direct air capture credit with geological storage received identical pool tokens at the same price. This is the condition that Akerlof identified in 1970^4^: when buyers cannot distinguish quality, sellers of low-quality goods capture a disproportionate market share, high-quality producers exit, and the market unravels. Manshadi et al.^5^ formalized this dynamic for carbon credit pools, showing that quality-blind pooling produces a separating equilibrium in which high-integrity credits are systematically excluded. The critical insight is that transaction transparency is not quality transparency --- and without the latter, the former may accelerate adverse selection by reducing friction for low-quality deposits.

Here we exploit the very transparency that failed to prevent BCT's collapse to produce, to our knowledge, the first depositor-level empirical test of adverse selection in any environmental market. Because every BCT deposit is a public blockchain event, we can reconstruct individual depositors' portfolios and ask the question that aggregate market data cannot answer: did depositors who held multiple credit types selectively deposit their lowest-quality credits while retaining their best? We complement this depositor-level analysis with a pool-level quality atlas spanning 34 market segments, providing the most comprehensive measurement of adverse selection severity across the voluntary carbon market to date.

Our analysis yields three principal contributions. First, we present depositor-level evidence of strategic quality selection: among [N_MULTI] depositors who held multiple TCO2 token types, [SELECTION_RATE]% deposited credits with lower mean quality than those they retained (mean quality differential $\Delta$ = [MEAN_DELTA], Wilcoxon $p$ < [P_WILCOXON], Cohen's $d$ = [COHENS_D]). This is the behavioural signature that Akerlof's theory predicts and Manshadi et al.'s model formalizes, observed at the individual agent level for the first time. Second, we corroborate depositor behaviour with pool-level evidence: BCT scores a Lemons Index of 0.724 (6.2 standard deviations above a random-assignment null model), while a systematic scan of 34 market segments reveals a quality spectrum from 0.076 (DACCS pool) to 0.759 (renewable energy pool), with quality-filtered pools consistently at the low end. Third, we demonstrate that quality gating --- enforcing a minimum quality threshold at deposit time --- reverses adverse selection: a BBB-grade gate reduces BCT's Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405, preventing 95% of historical deposits. Quality scores are derived from an open framework validated in a companion paper^6^ against the ICVCM Core Carbon Principles label (Cohen's $d$ = 1.80) and commercial agency ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901 with BeZero Carbon, $n$ = 27).

The irony is the story: blockchain made adverse selection visible, not prevented. The same on-chain records that failed to stop quality degradation now provide the most precisely documented case of adverse selection in any environmental market. The resolution is to make quality as transparent as transactions --- and to enforce that transparency at the protocol level.


## 2. Results

### 2.1 Depositor-level quality selection

To test whether BCT's quality degradation arose from strategic depositor behaviour rather than passive accumulation of low-quality supply, we reconstructed on-chain portfolios for all addresses that deposited TCO2 tokens into the BCT pool between October 2021 and December 2022 --- the period spanning BCT's launch, peak, and decline.

### 2.2 Pool-level quality degradation corroborates depositor behaviour

If depositors strategically selected against BCT, the pool's aggregate quality should be measurably worse than random. We quantify pool-level adverse selection using the Lemons Index (LI), defined as $L = 1 - (\bar{C} / 100)$, where $\bar{C}$ is the mean composite quality score across all credits in a pool (see Methods). A Lemons Index of 0 indicates perfect quality; 1 indicates complete quality failure.

BCT at its 2022 peak inventory of 43 credits exhibited a Lemons Index of 0.724, with a mean composite score of 27.6 out of 100 and zero credits rated A or above. Under a Monte Carlo null model --- random draws of 43 credits from the full 318-credit VCM dataset (10,000 iterations) --- the expected Lemons Index is 0.51 (SD = 0.026). BCT's observed LI of 0.724 falls 6.2 standard deviations above the null expectation, rejecting the hypothesis that BCT's composition arose by chance ($p < 10^{-9}$). This is the pool-level signature of the depositor-level behaviour documented above: the pool did not merely attract a random sample of the market; it attracted the worst.

A systematic scan of 34 market segments reveals the full quality spectrum (Fig. 3). At the adverse-selection extreme, pools defined by credit types that BCT disproportionately accumulated --- renewable energy (LI = 0.759, $n$ = 40), HFC-23 destruction (LI = 0.758, $n$ = 8), and legacy avoidance credits (LI = 0.691, $n$ = 101) --- score near or above BCT itself. At the quality-filtered end, pools restricted to engineered carbon dioxide removal --- DACCS (LI = 0.076, $n$ = 14), bio-oil geological injection (LI = 0.119, $n$ = 8), enhanced weathering (LI = 0.166, $n$ = 12) --- achieve Lemons Indices an order of magnitude lower.

Toucan's CHAR pool provides a natural experiment in quality filtering. CHAR restricts deposits to a narrow allowlist of biochar credits on Base, achieving a Lemons Index of 0.221 ($n$ = 12) --- less than one-third of BCT's. All 12 CHAR credits scored A or above. The 0.503-point gap between BCT and CHAR quantifies the value of quality curation: both pools operated on the same blockchain infrastructure and drew from overlapping registries, yet their quality profiles diverge by more than half the theoretical range of the index. CHAR achieves through category restriction what a generic quality gate would need to enforce through per-credit scoring --- by admitting only a credit type that inherently scores well on removal type, permanence, and MRV dimensions, the pool avoids adverse selection without requiring an on-chain quality oracle.

CarbonPlan's identification of "zombie" projects --- long-dormant credits that were newly issued specifically to sell into BCT^7^ --- provides the supply-side complement to our findings. Their analysis documented the project origination mechanism; our depositor-level analysis reveals the deposit-selection mechanism. Together, these constitute a pincer: BCT attracted new supply from the worst projects (supply-side) while existing holders selectively deposited their worst credits (depositor-side).

### 2.3 Quality gating as intervention

If adverse selection is driven by quality-blind deposit acceptance, the intervention is quality-informed deposit gates. We conducted counterfactual simulations on BCT's historical composition, progressively raising the minimum grade threshold and computing the resulting Lemons Index at each level (Fig. 5).

A minimum gate at BBB reduced BCT's Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 --- a 44% improvement --- while admitting only 2 of 43 credits (5%). A gate at BB produced an intermediate improvement (LI = 0.547, 7 credits admitted). No credits in BCT's 2022 inventory met the A threshold, meaning that any gate above BBB would have emptied the pool entirely. This result underscores the severity of BCT's adverse selection: the pool contained not a single credit that a moderately stringent quality filter would have accepted.

For the KlimaDAO kVCM inventory, which applies a curated allowlist on Base, a BBB gate reduces the Lemons Index from 0.519 to 0.273 while retaining 40% of credits (8 of 20). Unlike BCT, kVCM retains viable credits at every threshold --- evidence that curation at the pool-design level shifts the entire quality distribution upward.

The counterfactual is instructive: had BCT enforced a `meetsGrade(BBB)` check at deposit time --- a view function callable at zero marginal gas cost by any deposit contract --- it would have prevented 95% of historical deposits but maintained a Lemons Index of 0.405 rather than 0.724, potentially averting the confidence collapse that followed. This is not hypothetical: the CHAR pool's `checkEligible()` function already implements binary quality gating on Base. A companion paper^8^ describes the full on-chain implementation as a composable smart contract interface (ERC-CCQR) that generalizes binary curation to continuous, parameterizable quality thresholds.

### 2.4 Quality measurement validation

The depositor-level and pool-level analyses above depend on an underlying quality score. We briefly summarize the validation of the scoring framework, which is fully described in a companion paper^6^.

**CCP calibration.** We scored 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories and split by ICVCM Core Carbon Principles eligibility --- the most authoritative quality signal in the VCM. The framework produced a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible credits ($n$ = 165, mean ordinal grade = 2.69) and non-CCP credits ($n$ = 153, mean ordinal grade = 0.70). Cohen's $d$ = 1.80 (95% CI: 1.50--2.16), with a common language effect size of 91.4% --- a randomly chosen CCP-eligible credit outscored a randomly chosen non-CCP credit in more than nine out of ten pairings. This gap converges on the independently measured separation reported by Calyx Global^9^, an ICVCM-appointed rating agency, providing evidence that the framework's weight calibration is well-aligned with expert consensus.

**Commercial agency correlation.** Rank correlation with BeZero Carbon ratings across 27 cross-type projects yielded Spearman $\rho$ = +0.901 ($p$ < 0.001, permutation test), compared with a mean inter-agency correlation of +0.009 on a six-project REDD+ overlap sample^10^. The framework's agreement with commercial ratings exceeds the agreement among commercial agencies themselves.

**Inter-rater reliability.** An independent three-rater panel achieved Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600 at the grade level and ICC(2,$k$) = 0.993 on the continuous composite. Permanence ($\kappa$ = 0.684) and removal type ($\kappa$ = 0.585) --- the two highest-weighted dimensions --- showed the strongest agreement, meaning the framework's load-bearing dimensions are also its most reproducible. Additionality ($\kappa$ = 0.243) showed the weakest agreement, reflecting the fundamental difficulty of assessing counterfactual baselines --- a limitation shared by every carbon quality assessment system. The current panel uses Claude-family language models; multi-provider and human expert replication are planned but not yet completed.
