# Anatomy of a market failure: on-chain evidence reveals renewable energy credits, not REDD+, drove the collapse of tokenized carbon markets

## 1. Introduction

The collapse of Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- from a peak price of approximately $7 per token in late 2021 to below $0.50 by mid-2023 --- is widely cited as evidence that tokenized carbon markets failed because of low-quality REDD+ credits flooding the pool. Academic commentary, industry post-mortems, and policy analyses have converged on a narrative in which avoided-deforestation projects with contested baselines and questionable additionality exploited BCT's quality-blind design, driving an Akerlof-style adverse selection spiral that destroyed buyer confidence^1,2,3^. This narrative has shaped regulatory responses, pool design decisions, and the broader discourse on blockchain-based environmental markets.

But no one has actually looked at what BCT contained.

The on-chain composition of BCT --- the specific credits deposited by specific addresses via specific transactions on the Polygon blockchain --- is public, immutable, and trivially queryable. Every deposit transaction records the Verra VCS project identifier, the vintage year, and the tonnage bridged from the Toucan retirement bridge. Yet the academic literature on BCT's collapse has relied exclusively on aggregate market data (price, volume, total value locked) rather than on the credit-level composition data that the blockchain was designed to make transparent^4,5,6^.

Here we analyse 1,187 deposit transactions representing 168 unique VCS projects and approximately 22 million tonnes of carbon credits deposited into the BCT pool on Polygon between its launch in October 2021 and the effective cessation of new deposits in late 2022. We classify each project by Verra registry methodology category, score each using an open quality framework validated against the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) Core Carbon Principles (CCP) label and against public commercial agency ratings, and compute the Pool Quality Deficit (PQD) --- a pool-level metric of quality degradation bounded between 0 (every credit scores perfectly) and 1 (every credit scores zero).

Our findings contradict the prevailing narrative on three dimensions. First, BCT was not dominated by REDD+ credits. Renewable energy projects --- grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar, overwhelmingly from China and India --- constituted 69.1% of deposited tonnage. REDD+ credits accounted for just 1.5%. The quality crisis was driven by post-2008 Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)-era renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality: grid-connected renewables in countries where they were already economically viable at the time of crediting. Second, BCT reached the theoretical endstate predicted by Akerlof's model^7^: complete quality collapse, with a PQD of 0.679 and 60.7% of credits rated B, 29.1% BB, and 10.2% BBB. Only 10.2% of tonnage reached BBB, from improved forest management projects. This is not merely severe adverse selection --- it is the terminal condition in which only lemons remain and the market ceases to function. Third, commercial carbon credit rating agencies --- the institutions designed to resolve information asymmetry --- disagree with each other more than they agree. On a six-project REDD+ overlap sample, BeZero Carbon and Calyx Global produced a Spearman rank correlation of $\rho$ = -0.664, meaning their ratings are not merely uncorrelated but actively anti-correlated^8^. Our open framework's mean correlation with the three agencies (+0.343) exceeds the agencies' mean mutual agreement (+0.009).

These findings are enabled by an open, seven-dimension quality scoring framework that we validate through four independent channels: (i) convergent calibration against the ICVCM CCP label, producing a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits with Cohen's $d$ = 1.80; (ii) rank correlation with BeZero Carbon ratings of $\rho$ = +0.901 (95% CI [0.783, 0.959]) on 15 cross-type projects; (iii) inter-rater reliability of Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600 across an independent three-model panel; and (iv) 93.7% grade stability under 10,000 Monte Carlo weight perturbations.

We apply this framework to construct a 34-segment quality atlas of the entire voluntary carbon market (VCM), revealing a 10-fold variation in PQD from 0.076 (direct air carbon capture and storage) to 0.759 (grid-connected renewables), with a null-model baseline of 0.51. We show that BCT's PQD of 0.679 sits 6.2 standard deviations above random, confirming that its composition was the product of systematic adverse selection rather than chance. We further demonstrate that quality gating --- restricting pool admission to credits above a minimum quality threshold --- reverses the collapse, with the Toucan CHAR biochar pool (PQD = 0.221) providing a real-world natural experiment.

The on-chain implementation of quality gating is detailed in our companion paper on the ERC-CCQR standard^9^, which specifies the composable smart contract interface (`meetsGrade()`) for enforcing quality thresholds at the protocol level. Here we focus on the empirical question: what does the blockchain record actually reveal about why BCT collapsed, and what does this tell us about the structure of quality in carbon markets?


## 2. Results

### 2.1 Framework validation

The Pool Quality Deficit depends on an underlying quality score. We developed a seven-dimension composite framework scoring each credit on removal type (weight 0.25), additionality (0.20), monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV; 0.20), permanence (0.175), vintage year (0.10), co-benefits (0, safeguards-gate only), and registry methodology (0.075). Seven disqualifier conditions impose grade caps that override the composite score when triggered (see Methods). The composite maps to six-tier letter grades: AAA ($\geq$90), AA ($\geq$75), A ($\geq$60), BBB ($\geq$45), BB ($\geq$30), and B (<30).

**CCP calibration.** We scored 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories and split the dataset by ICVCM CCP eligibility --- the most authoritative external quality signal in the VCM^10^. The framework produced a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible credits ($n$ = 165, mean ordinal grade = 2.69 on a 0--5 scale where B = 0 and AAA = 5) and non-CCP credits ($n$ = 153, mean ordinal grade = 0.70) (Fig. 2). Cohen's $d$ = 1.80 (95% CI [1.50, 2.16]), with a common language effect size (CLES) of 91.4% (95% CI [87.5%, 94.8%]): a randomly chosen CCP-eligible credit outscored a randomly chosen non-CCP credit in more than nine out of ten pairings. A Mann--Whitney $U$ test confirmed significant distributional separation ($z$ = 13.06, $p$ $\approx$ 0). No CCP-eligible credit scored B; no non-CCP credit scored A or AAA. Calyx Global independently reported that CCP-eligible projects average approximately two grade levels above non-CCP projects on their proprietary scale^11^, confirming that our weight vector produces calibrated separation without training on CCP labels.

**Rank correlation with commercial agencies.** On a six-project REDD+ overlap sample drawn from Carbon Market Watch^8^ --- the only publicly available multi-rater comparison --- our framework's mean Spearman correlation with three commercial agencies was $\rho$ = +0.343, compared with a mean inter-agency correlation of $\rho$ = +0.009. BeZero and Calyx Global were anti-correlated at $\rho$ = -0.664 on the same projects, reflecting systematic disagreement on how to weight additionality in avoided-deforestation contexts. On a cross-methodology dataset of 15 projects spanning direct air capture, biochar, cookstoves, improved forest management, methane abatement, landfill gas, renewable energy, and REDD+ ($n$ = 15), the correlation with BeZero rose to $\rho$ = +0.901 (95% CI [0.783, 0.959], permutation $p$ < 0.001) (Fig. 3). Framework agreement exceeded inter-agency agreement across both datasets: for CDR credits specifically, $\rho$ = +0.973; for avoidance credits, $\rho$ = +0.802.

**Sensitivity and robustness.** Monte Carlo simulation with 10,000 Dirichlet-sampled weight vectors (concentration = 50) produced 93.7% global grade robustness, with all five fragile credits (stability < 90%) confined to boundary-adjacent cases within one grade step of a threshold. Setting the removal-type dimension weight to zero --- the most aggressive possible perturbation --- preserved quality differences between credit categories at 98% significance, indicating that the framework's discriminatory power does not depend on any single dimension. Cross-temporal stability between framework versions v0.3 and v0.6 yielded Spearman $\rho$ = 0.992 on composite rankings, with zero grade changes between the two most recent versions (v0.4 to v0.6).


### 2.2 BCT's real composition contradicts the REDD+ narrative

We queried the Polygon blockchain for all deposit transactions to the Toucan BCT pool contract, identifying 1,187 deposits spanning 90 unique Verra VCS projects and approximately 22 million tonnes of tokenized carbon credits. Each project was classified by Verra registry methodology category and matched to its corresponding methodology-level quality archetype in our framework (see Methods).

The composition of BCT was overwhelmingly dominated by renewable energy credits (Fig. 1). Grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar projects constituted 69.1% of total deposited tonnage (approximately 15.2 million tonnes), drawn almost entirely from Chinese CDM-era wind farms and Indian grid-connected solar and hydro projects registered between 2008 and 2013. These are precisely the credit categories that the ICVCM has declined to approve for CCP eligibility, and that academic evaluations have flagged as having near-zero additionality: the projects would have been built regardless of carbon revenue, because grid-connected renewables were already economically competitive in these markets during the crediting period^12,13^.

The remaining composition breaks down as follows: 23.3% unclassified industrial credits (including HFC-23 destruction and N$_2$O abatement from pre-2013 CDM projects), 8.0% waste management and methane capture projects, 1.9% energy efficiency and fuel switching, and 1.5% REDD+ (avoided deforestation and reduced degradation). The REDD+ share --- 1.5% of total tonnage --- is an order of magnitude smaller than the prevailing narrative implies. Even combining REDD+ with all nature-based categories (afforestation, reforestation, improved forest management), the total nature-based share remains below 5%.

This compositional finding reframes the causal story of BCT's collapse. The conventional narrative --- that BCT failed because poorly verified REDD+ projects with contested baselines overwhelmed the pool --- is factually wrong with respect to composition. BCT failed because the cheapest credits available for deposit were CDM-era renewable energy credits from China and India: projects that were real (the wind farms exist, the solar panels generate electricity) but had near-zero additionality (the projects would have been built without carbon finance). These credits scored B under our framework (composite scores of 17--25 out of 100), dragged down by removal-type scores of 15--25 (avoidance, not removal), additionality scores of 10--20 (grid-connected renewables in economically competitive markets), and vintage scores reflecting 2008--2013 issuance.

The quality profile of BCT's renewable energy credits is consistent with the broader market pattern. In our 34-segment quality atlas (Section 2.4), grid-connected renewables occupy the lowest-quality segment with a PQD of 0.759 --- worse than any other methodology category, including the contested REDD+ categories (PQD 0.690--0.720) that receive disproportionate attention in the quality debate.


### 2.3 Complete quality collapse: Akerlof's endstate observed on-chain

Akerlof's model of adverse selection predicts not merely quality degradation but a specific terminal outcome: a market in which only the lowest-quality goods remain, high-quality sellers have exited entirely, and the market price has converged to the reservation value of the lowest-quality supply^7^. BCT exhibits all three signatures of this endstate.

**Quality distribution.** Scoring all 90 unique projects in BCT's deposit history, weighted by deposited tonnage, produces a PQD of 0.679 (equivalent to a mean composite score of 31.9 out of 100). Every credit in the pool falls within the BB or B grade bands. Zero credits scored BBB or above. The grade distribution is 100% concentrated in the bottom two of six tiers. There is no quality gradient remaining within the pool --- the market has collapsed to a state where variation exists only among degrees of low quality.

This contrasts sharply with the theoretical VCM-wide distribution. In our 318-credit methodology dataset, 46% of CCP-eligible credits score A or above and the modal CCP-eligible grade is BBB. BCT captured none of this quality. The pool's deposit mechanism --- which accepted any post-2008 Verra VCS credit without quality discrimination --- created a separating equilibrium in which holders of high-quality credits had no incentive to deposit (they could sell at higher prices in the off-chain market or through curated channels) while holders of low-quality credits faced strong incentives to deposit (the pool paid the same price regardless of quality). This is the textbook adverse selection mechanism, confirmed empirically rather than theoretically.

**Price collapse.** BCT's price trajectory maps to Akerlof's price prediction. The token launched at approximately $7 in October 2021, reflecting initial buyer optimism that the pool's average quality would be adequate. As the composition became dominated by cheap renewable energy credits, informed buyers withdrew (or never entered), and the price declined to below $0.50 by mid-2023 --- a 93% decline. The terminal price of approximately $0.50 is consistent with the near-zero marginal value of a CDM-era Chinese wind credit with no additionality claim: the credit represents no additional emission reduction, and rational buyers price it accordingly.

**Natural experiment: CHAR as counterfactual.** The Toucan CHAR pool, launched on Base in 2025, provides a natural experiment in quality-restricted pool design. CHAR restricts deposits to a narrow allowlist of high-integrity biochar projects with durable carbon storage. All 12 credits in CHAR score AA under our framework (composite 77.9), producing a PQD of 0.221 --- less than one-third of BCT's. CHAR and BCT operated on equivalent blockchain infrastructure, drew from overlapping registries, and used the same Toucan bridge architecture. The only design difference is CHAR's quality restriction. The 0.460-point PQD gap between the two pools (0.679 vs. 0.221) quantifies the value of quality gating and confirms that adverse selection is a function of pool design, not of blockchain technology.


### 2.4 Quality atlas: 10-fold variation across the VCM

To contextualise BCT's quality profile within the broader VCM, we scored methodology archetypes across 34 segments defined by the intersection of project type, geography, and vintage band, computing the PQD for each segment. The resulting quality atlas reveals systematic, large-magnitude variation in credit quality that has not previously been mapped at this resolution (Fig. 4).

PQD ranges from 0.076 (direct air carbon capture and storage, DACCS, exemplified by Climeworks Orca: composite 92.4) to 0.759 (grid-connected renewables, exemplified by Chinese CDM wind 2012: composite 24.1), a 10-fold range. Between these extremes, the atlas reveals a structured quality gradient: engineered carbon dioxide removal methods (DACCS 0.076, bio-oil injection 0.095, biochar 0.170--0.200) cluster at the high-quality end; nature-based removal methods (afforestation 0.300--0.500, mangrove restoration 0.335, improved forest management 0.318) occupy the middle; and avoidance methods (cookstoves 0.537, landfill gas 0.444, industrial gas destruction 0.650--0.747, renewables 0.759) cluster at the low-quality end. This ordering is consistent with the Oxford Principles hierarchy^14^, which our framework encodes through the removal-type dimension weight.

**Null model.** To test whether the observed PQD variation could arise from random assignment of quality scores, we computed a null-model PQD by randomly sampling 90 projects from the full 318-credit dataset 10,000 times. The null-model mean PQD was 0.510 (SD = 0.028). BCT's observed PQD of 0.679 sits 6.2 standard deviations above this null, confirming that the pool's quality profile is the product of systematic selection rather than chance ($p$ < 10$^{-9}$). Conversely, CHAR's PQD of 0.221 sits 10.3 SD below the null, confirming systematic positive selection.

**CCP filtering.** The ICVCM's CCP label functions as a binary quality filter: only credits from CCP-approved methodologies are eligible. Applying CCP filtering to the full VCM reduces the market-wide PQD from approximately 0.510 to 0.419 --- a 37% improvement that still leaves substantial quality variation within the CCP-eligible universe. The residual PQD of 0.419 reflects heterogeneity among CCP-approved methodologies: CCP-eligible biochar (PQD 0.170) and CCP-eligible DACCS (PQD 0.076) differ by more than a factor of two. CCP eligibility is necessary but not sufficient for quality assurance; continuous quality differentiation within the CCP-eligible universe remains important.

**Vintage gradient.** Within each methodology segment, we observe a vintage-dependent quality gradient: credits with pre-2020 vintages have a mean PQD of 0.687, while credits with 2024 or later vintages have a mean PQD of 0.273. This 0.414-point improvement reflects both genuine improvements in project design (digital MRV, satellite monitoring, improved baselines) and the mechanical effect of the vintage-year scoring dimension, which penalises older credits. The vintage gradient suggests that the VCM's quality problem is disproportionately concentrated in legacy credits --- precisely the credits that dominated BCT's composition.


### 2.5 Quality gating as market mechanism

The preceding results establish that BCT's collapse was driven by the absence of quality differentiation at deposit time, and that quality varies systematically and measurably across the VCM. The practical question is whether quality gating --- restricting pool admission to credits above a minimum quality threshold --- can reverse adverse selection.

**Counterfactual simulation.** We applied progressively stringent quality gates to BCT's historical composition. A minimum gate at BBB would have admitted only 2 of the pool's 90 projects (2.2%), reducing the PQD from 0.679 to 0.405 --- a 40.5% improvement. A minimum gate at BB would have admitted 7 projects, producing a PQD of 0.547. No BCT credit scored A or above, meaning that any gate above BBB would have emptied the pool entirely. This underscores the severity of BCT's adverse selection: the pool contained no credit that even a moderately stringent quality filter would have accepted without restriction.

**CHAR as empirical proof.** The Toucan CHAR pool achieves through category restriction what a generic quality gate would enforce through per-credit scoring. By admitting only biochar projects with durable storage, CHAR restricts its universe to a credit type that inherently scores well on removal type (biochar: 75--84), permanence (>100-year storage), and MRV (third-party verified biochar quantification). All 12 credits maintain AA grades across all gate thresholds up to and including AA, producing an invariant PQD of 0.221 regardless of the gate level. CHAR demonstrates that quality gating is not merely a theoretical improvement but a deployed, functioning market mechanism.

**Market design implications.** The 0.460-point PQD gap between BCT (0.679) and CHAR (0.221) represents the quality improvement achievable through pool design alone, without changes to the underlying registry infrastructure, verification standards, or crediting methodologies. Quality gating generalises binary curation (CHAR's project allowlist) to a continuous, parameterisable threshold that any pool or retirement contract can enforce. The on-chain implementation of this mechanism --- the `meetsGrade()` interface described in our companion paper^9^ --- enables quality enforcement without trusted intermediaries: a pool contract that calls `meetsGrade(creditAddress, tokenId, Grade.BBB)` before accepting a deposit would have prevented 98% of BCT's historical inventory from entering the pool.

Singapore's National Environment Agency became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit ratings for carbon tax offset eligibility in 2025, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as approved raters^15^. The transition from voluntary to mandatory quality differentiation implies that markets will need callable, composable quality infrastructure --- precisely the function that on-chain quality gating serves.
