# Anatomy of a market failure: on-chain evidence reveals renewable energy credits, not REDD+, drove the collapse of tokenized carbon markets

## 1. Introduction

The collapse of Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- from a peak price of approximately $7 per token in late 2021 to below $0.50 by mid-2023 --- is widely cited as evidence that tokenized carbon markets failed because of low-quality REDD+ credits flooding the pool. Academic commentary, industry post-mortems, and policy analyses have converged on a narrative in which avoided-deforestation projects with contested baselines and questionable additionality exploited BCT's quality-blind design, driving a quality collapse that destroyed buyer confidence^1,2,3^. This narrative has shaped regulatory responses, pool design decisions, and the broader discourse on blockchain-based environmental markets.

But no one has actually looked at what BCT contained.

The on-chain composition of BCT --- the specific credits deposited by specific addresses via specific transactions on the Polygon blockchain --- is public, immutable, and trivially queryable. Every deposit transaction records the Verra VCS project identifier, the vintage year, and the tonnage bridged from the Toucan retirement bridge. Yet the academic literature on BCT's collapse has relied exclusively on aggregate market data (price, volume, total value locked) rather than on the credit-level composition data that the blockchain was designed to make transparent^4,5,6^.

Here we analyse 1,187 deposit transactions representing 168 unique VCS projects and approximately 22 million tonnes of carbon credits deposited into the BCT pool on Polygon between its launch in October 2021 and the effective cessation of new deposits in late 2022. We classify each project by Verra registry methodology category, score each using an open quality framework validated against the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) Core Carbon Principles (CCP) label and against public commercial agency ratings, and compute the Pool Quality Deficit (PQD) --- a pool-level metric of quality degradation bounded between 0 (every credit scores perfectly) and 1 (every credit scores zero).

Our findings contradict the prevailing narrative on three dimensions. First, BCT was not dominated by REDD+ credits. Renewable energy projects --- grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar, overwhelmingly from China and India --- constituted 69.1% of deposited tonnage. REDD+ credits accounted for just 4.2%. The quality crisis was driven by post-2008 Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)-era renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality: grid-connected renewables in countries where they were already economically viable at the time of crediting. Second, BCT's composition represents 1.87$\times$ over-selection of renewables relative to the Verra VCS base rate of approximately 37% (binomial $p$ < 10$^{-187}$), confirming genuine Gresham-type selection rather than passive market-supply reflection. The pool's PQD of 0.679, with 60.7% B, 29.1% BB, and 10.2% BBB, constitutes a low-type pooling equilibrium in which the uniform-price mechanism has driven out all high-quality supply. Third, commercial carbon credit rating agencies --- the institutions designed to resolve information asymmetry --- disagree with each other more than they agree. On a six-project REDD+ overlap sample, BeZero Carbon and Calyx Global produced a Spearman rank correlation of $\rho$ = -0.664, meaning their ratings are not merely uncorrelated but actively anti-correlated^8^. Our open framework's mean correlation with the three agencies (+0.343) exceeds the agencies' mean mutual agreement (+0.009).

These findings are enabled by an open, seven-dimension quality scoring framework that we validate through four independent channels: (i) convergent calibration against the ICVCM CCP label, producing a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits with Cohen's $d$ = 1.80; (ii) rank correlation with BeZero Carbon ratings of $\rho$ = +0.901 on 27 cross-type projects spanning 12 methodology categories; (iii) inter-rater reliability of Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600 across an independent three-model panel; and (iv) 93.7% grade stability under 10,000 Monte Carlo weight perturbations.

We apply this framework to construct a 34-segment quality atlas of the VCM (10-fold PQD variation, 0.076 to 0.759) and test BCT's composition against the Verra VCS base rate: BCT's renewable share (69.1%) is 1.87$\times$ the registry base rate of 37% (binomial $p$ < 10$^{-187}$), confirming genuine over-selection. Temporal quality degradation ($\rho$ = $-$0.24) is present before the Terra/LUNA crash ($\rho$ = $-$0.13, $p$ = 4.0 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$, $n$ = 792) and not attributable to KlimaDAO (zero wallet overlap), though cluster-robust inference indicates the temporal signal is suggestive rather than definitive. Quality-gated pools exhibit descriptively better outcomes: BCT (PQD 0.679) $\rightarrow$ NCT (0.601) $\rightarrow$ CHAR (0.221), though these comparisons are observational.

The on-chain implementation of quality gating is detailed in our companion paper on the ERC-CCQR standard^9^, which specifies the composable smart contract interface (`meetsGrade()`) for enforcing quality thresholds at the protocol level. Here we focus on the empirical question: what does the blockchain record actually reveal about why BCT collapsed, and what does this tell us about the structure of quality in carbon markets?


## 2. Results

### 2.1 Framework validation

The Pool Quality Deficit depends on an underlying quality score. We developed a seven-dimension composite framework scoring each credit on removal type (weight 0.25), additionality (0.20), monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV; 0.20), permanence (0.175), vintage year (0.10), co-benefits (0, safeguards-gate only), and registry methodology (0.075). Seven disqualifier conditions impose grade caps that override the composite score when triggered (see Methods). The composite maps to six-tier letter grades: AAA ($\geq$90), AA ($\geq$75), A ($\geq$60), BBB ($\geq$45), BB ($\geq$30), and B (<30).

**CCP calibration.** We scored 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories and split the dataset by ICVCM CCP eligibility --- the most authoritative external quality signal in the VCM^10^. The framework produced a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible credits ($n$ = 165, mean ordinal grade = 2.69 on a 0--5 scale where B = 0 and AAA = 5) and non-CCP credits ($n$ = 153, mean ordinal grade = 0.70) (Fig. 2). Cohen's $d$ = 1.80 (95% CI [1.50, 2.16]), with a common language effect size (CLES) of 91.4% (95% CI [87.5%, 94.8%]): a randomly chosen CCP-eligible credit outscored a randomly chosen non-CCP credit in more than nine out of ten pairings. A Mann--Whitney $U$ test confirmed significant distributional separation ($z$ = 13.06, $p$ $\approx$ 0). No CCP-eligible credit scored B; no non-CCP credit scored A or AAA. Calyx Global independently reported that CCP-eligible projects average approximately two grade levels above non-CCP projects on their proprietary scale^11^, confirming that our weight vector produces calibrated separation without training on CCP labels.

**Rank correlation with commercial agencies.** On a six-project REDD+ overlap sample drawn from Carbon Market Watch^8^ --- the only publicly available multi-rater comparison --- our framework's mean Spearman correlation with three commercial agencies was $\rho$ = +0.343, compared with a mean inter-agency correlation of $\rho$ = +0.009. BeZero and Calyx Global were anti-correlated at $\rho$ = -0.664 on the same projects, reflecting systematic disagreement on how to weight additionality in avoided-deforestation contexts. On an expanded cross-methodology dataset of 30 projects spanning 12 types --- direct air capture, biochar, enhanced weathering, cookstoves, improved forest management, methane abatement, landfill gas, ODS destruction, renewable energy, jurisdictional REDD+, and REDD+ --- the correlation with BeZero on the 27 projects with paired ratings rose to $\rho$ = +0.901 (Kendall $\tau$-b = 0.821, 100% within $\pm$1 grade) (Fig. 3). Framework agreement exceeded inter-agency agreement across both datasets: for CDR credits specifically, $\rho$ = +0.973; for avoidance credits, $\rho$ = +0.802.

**Sensitivity.** Monte Carlo simulation with 10,000 Dirichlet-sampled weight vectors produced 93.7% global grade robustness. Setting the removal-type weight to zero preserved quality differences at 98% significance. Cross-temporal stability between framework versions v0.3 and v0.6 yielded $\rho$ = 0.992.


### 2.2 BCT's real composition contradicts the REDD+ narrative

We queried the Polygon blockchain for all deposit transactions to the Toucan BCT pool contract, identifying 1,187 deposits spanning 168 unique Verra VCS projects and approximately 22 million tonnes of tokenized carbon credits. Each project was classified by Verra registry methodology category and matched to its corresponding methodology-level quality archetype in our framework (see Methods).

The composition of BCT was overwhelmingly dominated by renewable energy credits (Fig. 1). Grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar projects constituted 69.1% of total deposited tonnage (approximately 15.2 million tonnes), drawn almost entirely from Chinese CDM-era wind farms and Indian grid-connected solar and hydro projects registered between 2008 and 2013. These are precisely the credit categories that the ICVCM has declined to approve for CCP eligibility, and that academic evaluations have flagged as having near-zero additionality: the projects would have been built regardless of carbon revenue, because grid-connected renewables were already economically competitive in these markets during the crediting period^12,13^.

The remaining composition breaks down as follows: fossil fuel switching 10.4%, waste management and methane capture 5.5%, REDD+ 4.2%, afforestation/reforestation (ARR) 3.9%, industrial gas destruction (HFC-23, N$_2$O) 3.1%, improved forest management (IFM) 2.0%, energy efficiency 0.9%, and other categories below 1%. The REDD+ share --- 4.2% of total tonnage --- is more than 16 times smaller than the renewable energy share. Even combining all nature-based categories (REDD+, ARR, IFM, agriculture), the total nature-based share is 10.3%, compared with 69.1% for renewable energy alone.

This reframes the collapse: BCT failed because CDM-era renewable energy credits --- real projects with near-zero additionality --- were the cheapest credits available for deposit. These scored B (composite 17--25), dragged down by avoidance-type removal, near-zero additionality in economically competitive markets, and 2008--2013 vintages. In our 34-segment quality atlas (Section 2.4), grid-connected renewables occupy the lowest-quality segment (PQD 0.759) --- worse than the contested REDD+ categories (PQD 0.690--0.720).


### 2.3 Low-type pooling equilibrium: Gresham dynamics in a uniform-price pool

Gresham's law --- "bad money drives out good" --- predicts that when a market assigns a uniform price to goods of heterogeneous quality, low-quality goods drive out high-quality ones. BCT's permissionless design, which priced all BCT-eligible credits identically at 1 BCT token per tonne regardless of quality, creates the structural conditions for this dynamic. The empirical question is whether BCT's composition reflects Gresham-type selection or merely the shape of the available credit supply.

**Quality distribution.** Scoring all 345 unique TCO2 tokens (168 projects) in BCT's deposit history (see Methods, Extended scoring), weighted by deposited tonnage, produces a PQD of 0.679 (volume-weighted mean composite 32.1 out of 100). The grade distribution: 60.7% B, 29.1% BB, 10.2% BBB, 0% A or above. BCT's terminal price of approximately $0.50 is consistent with rational discounting to the reservation value of the dominant credit type: a CDM-era Chinese wind credit with documented near-zero additionality.

**Base-rate comparison against the Verra VCS registry.** BCT's renewable share (69.1%) could, in principle, reflect the shape of the VCS-eligible universe rather than selective deposit. We obtained Verra VCS issuance data from MSCI (2023) and Ecosystem Marketplace (2023) indicating that renewable energy credits constitute approximately 37% of total VCS issuance by volume (sensitivity range 26--48%). BCT's renewable share is 1.87$\times$ the VCS base rate (95% CI 1.44--2.66$\times$; binomial $p$ < 10$^{-187}$). Even under conservative assumptions (VCS renewable base rate as high as 48%), BCT over-selects at 1.44$\times$ ($p$ < 10$^{-30}$). Conversely, REDD+ is under-represented at 0.20$\times$ the VCS base rate (BCT 4.2% vs. VCS $\sim$17%). These selection coefficients confirm that BCT's composition is not a passive reflection of market supply but the product of genuine quality-price arbitrage: low-quality renewables were cheaper to acquire off-chain than BCT's uniform pool price, while higher-quality nature-based credits commanded off-chain premia that exceeded the BCT price.

**Price collapse.** BCT's price trajectory matches Gresham's prediction. The token launched at approximately $7 in October 2021, reflecting initial buyer optimism. As the composition became dominated by cheap renewable energy credits, informed buyers withdrew (or never entered), and the price declined to below $0.50 by mid-2023 --- a 93% decline.

**Cross-pool comparison.** The Toucan NCT pool (nature-based filter, same blockchain) has a PQD of 0.601; the Toucan CHAR pool (biochar allowlist) has a PQD of 0.221. These pools are descriptively better than BCT. However, the BCT--NCT comparison cannot support causal inference: 88.6% of NCT's tokens also appear in BCT, 19 wallets deposited to both pools, and NCT was designed after BCT's problems were observed. Furthermore, BCT's nature-based deposits (mean quality 44.4) actually exceed NCT's pool-wide mean (40.0), indicating that the dose--response pattern across pools reflects type composition rather than within-type quality improvement. We therefore present the cross-pool gradient as descriptive evidence only, not causal identification. Toucan's monopoly on permissionless carbon tokenization in 2021--2022 means no independent control pool exists for rigorous causal inference.

**CHAR as proof of concept.** The CHAR pool demonstrates that restricting the eligible universe to high-quality credit types prevents quality collapse: all 12 credits score AA (composite 77.9), PQD = 0.221. CHAR and BCT use equivalent Toucan infrastructure; the sole difference is CHAR's category restriction. The 0.460-point PQD gap between BCT and CHAR quantifies the quality improvement achievable through pool design.


### 2.4 Quality atlas: 10-fold variation across the VCM

To contextualise BCT's quality profile within the broader VCM, we scored methodology archetypes across 34 segments defined by the intersection of project type, geography, and vintage band, computing the PQD for each segment. The resulting quality atlas reveals systematic, large-magnitude variation in credit quality that has not previously been mapped at this resolution (Fig. 4).

PQD ranges from 0.076 (direct air carbon capture and storage, DACCS, exemplified by Climeworks Orca: composite 92.4) to 0.759 (grid-connected renewables, exemplified by Chinese CDM wind 2012: composite 24.1), a 10-fold range. Between these extremes, the atlas reveals a structured quality gradient: engineered carbon dioxide removal methods (DACCS 0.076, bio-oil injection 0.095, biochar 0.170--0.200) cluster at the high-quality end; nature-based removal methods (afforestation 0.300--0.500, mangrove restoration 0.335, improved forest management 0.318) occupy the middle; and avoidance methods (cookstoves 0.537, landfill gas 0.444, industrial gas destruction 0.650--0.747, renewables 0.759) cluster at the low-quality end. This ordering is consistent with the Oxford Principles hierarchy^14^, which our framework encodes through the removal-type dimension weight.

**Null model.** To test whether tonnage allocation within BCT was biased toward the lowest-quality tokens, we permuted quality scores across the 161 BCT-eligible TCO2 tokens while preserving the tonnage distribution (100,000 iterations; see Methods). The null produced mean 32.30 (SD 0.75); BCT's observed volume-weighted mean of 31.71 yields $z$ = $-$0.64 ($p$ = 0.27) --- not significant. The Spearman correlation between token quality and total deposited tonnes is $\rho$ = $-$0.088 ($p$ = 0.27), confirming no tonnage bias toward lower-quality tokens. This is an important negative: *within* BCT's eligible universe, depositors do not further concentrate on the worst tokens. The eligible universe itself is uniformly low-quality (equal-weighted mean 32.3, range 27.3--52.0), leaving little room for within-pool selection. The adverse selection in BCT therefore operates primarily at the *token eligibility stage* --- what types of credits get tokenized and bridged to the pool --- rather than through selective deposit behaviour within the pool. Conversely, CHAR's PQD of 0.221 demonstrates that restricting the eligible universe to high-quality credit types prevents quality collapse entirely.

**CCP filtering and vintage gradient.** Applying the ICVCM CCP label as a binary filter reduces the VCM-wide PQD from 0.510 to 0.419, but substantial within-CCP heterogeneity persists (CCP-eligible biochar 0.170 vs. CCP-eligible DACCS 0.076). A vintage gradient (pre-2020 PQD 0.687, 2024+ PQD 0.273) confirms that the quality problem is concentrated in legacy credits --- the credits that dominated BCT.


### 2.5 Quality gating as market mechanism

The preceding results establish that BCT's collapse was driven by the absence of quality differentiation at deposit time, and that quality varies systematically and measurably across the VCM. The practical question is whether quality gating --- restricting pool admission to credits above a minimum quality threshold --- can reverse adverse selection.

**Counterfactual simulation.** We applied progressively stringent quality gates to BCT's historical composition. A minimum gate at BBB would have admitted only 2 of the pool's 168 projects (1.2%), reducing the PQD from 0.679 to 0.405 --- a 40.5% improvement. A minimum gate at BB would have admitted 7 projects, producing a PQD of 0.547. No BCT credit scored A or above, meaning that any gate above BBB would have emptied the pool entirely. This underscores the severity of BCT's adverse selection: the pool contained no credit that even a moderately stringent quality filter would have accepted without restriction.

**Market design implications.** The 0.460-point PQD gap between BCT (0.679) and CHAR (0.221) quantifies the quality improvement achievable through pool design alone. Quality gating generalises binary curation to a continuous, parameterisable threshold: a pool contract calling `meetsGrade(creditAddress, tokenId, Grade.BBB)` would have prevented 97.8% of BCT's historical inventory from entering^9^. Singapore's 2025 mandate for commercial carbon credit ratings^15^ signals the transition from voluntary to mandatory quality differentiation --- precisely the function that composable on-chain quality infrastructure serves.


### 2.6 Depositor-level evidence: temporal dynamics and robustness

To characterise the dynamics of BCT's quality profile, we scored all 345 unique TCO2 tokens (extended from the initial 161; see Methods) and analysed all 1,187 deposit events.

**Temporal quality decline.** Deposit quality declines over BCT's operating life: Spearman $\rho$ = $-$0.24 ($n$ = 1,187; asymptotic $p$ < 10$^{-16}$, permutation $p$ < 0.0001, both surviving Benjamini--Hochberg FDR at $\alpha$ = 0.05). By quartiles: Q1 mean 32.5 (53% B-grade), Q4 mean 28.6 (99% B-grade). The decline is present in the pre-Terra/LUNA subsample ($\rho$ = $-$0.13, permutation $p$ = 0.0001, $n$ = 792 deposits before May 2022), confirming it is not an artefact of the May 2022 or November 2022 crypto market shocks. However, a cluster-robust bootstrap (resampling at the TCO2-token level rather than the deposit level, 10,000 iterations) produces a wider 95% CI of [$-$0.32, +0.02] that marginally includes zero, reflecting the fact that the 1,187 deposits map to only 345 unique tokens with identical per-token scores. The temporal decline should therefore be interpreted as suggestive rather than definitive; the effective sample size for temporal inference is closer to the 345 unique tokens than the 1,187 deposits.

**Within-type decomposition.** The decline is driven primarily by the renewable segment. However, we note an important methodological caveat: the composite score includes a vintage-year dimension (weight 0.10), and vintage is strongly correlated with deposit time ($\rho$ = $-$0.79 within renewables; late deposits are systematically older vintages). A robustness check using a vintage-free composite --- redistributing the 0.10 vintage weight across the remaining dimensions --- shows that the temporal correlation in renewables reverses to $\rho$ = +0.24 (positive). This indicates that the observed decline is driven by the vintage component of the score, not by within-vintage quality changes across the other five dimensions (removal type, additionality, permanence, MRV, registry methodology). We report the headline $\rho$ = $-$0.24 using the full composite for consistency with the rest of the paper but emphasise that the temporal signal is primarily a vintage-composition effect: late-period BCT deposits drew from progressively older credit vintages.

**Compositional shift.** The renewable share of tonnage rises from 67% (Q1) to 99.5% (Q4). Token diversity collapses from 24 distinct composite scores in Q1 to 2 in Q4. Higher-quality credit types (IFM, Waste/Methane, ARR) ceased depositing earlier (median deposit blocks 20.4M--21.7M) than renewables (median 32.6M). We cannot distinguish whether this pattern reflects strategic exit by higher-quality suppliers (Gresham dynamics) or differential tokenization supply --- types with smaller bridgeable supply may simply saturate earlier. Both mechanisms predict the same observable pattern; distinguishing them would require redemption-side and off-chain price data we do not have.

**Depositor concentration.** BCT's deposits are concentrated among a small number of wallets (Gini = 0.940; top 10 of 509 wallets = 50% of tonnage; effective $N$ = 1/HHI = 26.4). The top 20 depositors by tonnage (71.9% of volume) deposited at a volume-weighted mean quality of 30.3, while the remaining 459 depositors (28.1% of volume) deposited at 35.3. A two-sided Mann--Whitney $U$ test on deposit-level composites detects a small but significant difference ($p$ = 8.6 $\times$ 10$^{-5}$, FDR-adjusted $p$ = 1.7 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$, rank-biserial $r$ = $-$0.17): large depositors deposited marginally higher quality at the per-deposit level. The volume-weighted gap (5.0 points in the opposite direction) does not reach significance under permutation ($p$ = 0.082, 10,000 iterations), suggesting the tonnage-weighted difference is driven by a few high-volume tokens rather than systematic depositor-level strategy.

**External validation.** Seven BCT projects (385,643 tonnes) have public BeZero ratings. The correlation between our framework and BeZero on this subset is directionally consistent ($\rho$ = 0.77, $p$ = 0.073; underpowered at $n$ = 6 paired scores). Under BeZero's scale, 5 of 7 BCT projects fall below investment grade. The average BeZero score for BCT-overlapping projects (30.7) is 17.4 points below BeZero's broader rated universe (48.1), directionally confirming the adverse quality profile independently of our scoring framework. We note that the $p$ = 0.073 result does not reach significance at $\alpha$ = 0.05 and should be interpreted as suggestive rather than confirmatory.
