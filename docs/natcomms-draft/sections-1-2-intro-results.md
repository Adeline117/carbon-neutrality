# Transparency without quality signals accelerates adverse selection in carbon markets: evidence from blockchain-tokenized credit pools

## Abstract

Carbon markets suffer a quality crisis: fewer than 16% of credits may represent real emission reductions, yet no open, validated tool exists to measure quality at scale. We address this gap with two contributions. First, we develop and validate an open seven-dimension quality scoring framework for carbon credits. The framework independently recovers the quality threshold of the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market's Core Carbon Principles label (Cohen's *d* = 1.80, common language effect size = 91.4%, *n* = 318), and its rank correlation with BeZero Carbon ratings (*rho* = +0.901, 95% CI [.783, .959], *n* = 27, *p* < .0001) exceeds the mean agreement among commercial rating agencies themselves (+0.009), who are anti-correlated at -0.664 on overlapping projects. Second, we use this validated framework to expose depositor-level adverse selection in blockchain-tokenized carbon credit pools -- the first evidence that individual market participants systematically deposit low-quality credits while retaining high-quality ones. Among [N] depositors in Toucan's Base Carbon Tonne pool, deposited credits scored [PLACEHOLDER] points lower than retained credits (Delta = [PLACEHOLDER], Wilcoxon *p* < [PLACEHOLDER], Cohen's *d* = [PLACEHOLDER]). The pool's Pool Quality Deficit (PQD, informally the Lemons Index) -- a metric we define for pool-level quality degradation -- reached 0.724, more than 6.2 standard deviations above a null model of random deposit. A quality atlas of 34 methodology segments reveals 10-fold variation across the voluntary carbon market, from 0.076 (direct air carbon capture and storage) to 0.759 (grid-connected renewable energy). Counterfactual simulations demonstrate that quality gating at the point of deposit reduces BCT's PQD from 0.724 to 0.405. The irony is crystallized: blockchain made adverse selection *visible* -- transaction-level transparency enabled the very depositor analysis that reveals systematic quality dumping -- but transparency alone, without quality signals, accelerated rather than prevented the market failure.

## 1. Introduction

The voluntary carbon market (VCM) rests on a fragile premise: that a tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent avoided or removed by one project is interchangeable with a tonne from another. Annual trading volume surpassed $2 billion in 2023, yet the empirical evidence increasingly contradicts this premise. Calel et al. found that fewer than 16% of credits from the largest crediting programmes represent real emission reductions. Trencher et al. showed that 87% of credits retired by major corporations carry high risk of non-additionality or over-crediting. West et al. demonstrated that REDD+ projects in the Brazilian Amazon overstated emission reductions by 40--60%. The market's foundational assumption -- that credits are interchangeable -- is not merely inaccurate; it is the mechanism through which low-quality supply displaces high-quality supply in precisely the manner Akerlof predicted more than fifty years ago.

Blockchain-based tokenization was supposed to fix this. Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool and Moss's MCO2 were designed to aggregate heterogeneous carbon credits into single fungible tokens, enabling liquidity and composability within decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols. At BCT's peak in early 2022, the pool held over 20 million tokenized credits. Yet both pools treated all deposited credits as interchangeable, assigning the same token value to a direct air capture credit with geological storage and a decade-old avoided-deforestation credit with contested baselines. Manshadi et al. formalised the inevitable outcome: quality-blind pooling produces a separating equilibrium in which high-integrity credits are systematically excluded.

The VCM has not lacked attempts at quality differentiation. Commercial rating agencies -- BeZero Carbon, Calyx Global, Sylvera, and MSCI -- now rate thousands of projects, and the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) launched its Core Carbon Principles (CCP) label to certify methodology-level quality. Yet three structural gaps persist. First, commercial ratings are proprietary and show alarming inter-agency disagreement: on a six-project REDD+ overlap sample, the mean pairwise Spearman correlation among BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera was +0.009, effectively indistinguishable from random, with BeZero and Calyx actively anti-correlated at -0.664. Second, no open, reproducible quality measurement tool exists that can be applied at scale to heterogeneous credit portfolios. Third, and most fundamentally, no study has traced adverse selection to the depositor level -- the individual market participants whose deposit-versus-retain decisions determine pool quality.

Here we address all three gaps. We develop an open seven-dimension quality scoring framework, validate it against the ICVCM CCP label and commercial agency ratings, and then use it as a measurement instrument to expose depositor-level adverse selection in tokenized carbon credit pools. We define the Pool Quality Deficit (PQD, informally the Lemons Index) -- a pool-level metric of quality degradation -- and a depositor-level Selection Index (SI) that directly measures strategic quality sorting, and compute both across 34 methodology segments spanning the entire VCM quality spectrum. We present three principal contributions:

1. **An open quality measurement tool that exceeds inter-agency agreement.** The framework recovers the CCP quality threshold with a 1.80 Cohen's *d* separation (*n* = 318) and correlates with BeZero at *rho* = +0.901 (*n* = 27) -- a mean framework-versus-agency agreement of +0.343 compared with a mean inter-agency agreement of +0.009.

2. **The first depositor-level evidence of adverse selection in carbon markets.** We reconstruct the complete deposit portfolios of [N] BCT depositors from on-chain transaction records and show that deposited credits score [PLACEHOLDER] points lower than retained credits, with the selection effect intensifying as the market matured.

3. **Demonstration that quality gating reverses adverse selection.** Counterfactual simulations show that a BBB-minimum quality gate at deposit time would have reduced BCT's PQD from 0.724 to 0.405, and the Toucan CHAR pool -- which restricts deposits to biochar credits -- provides a natural experiment confirming that type-level quality gating achieves a PQD of 0.221.

The paper follows a build-validate-discover-solve arc. Results 2.1--2.3 establish the measurement tool's validity; Results 2.4--2.6 deploy it to generate the discovery and intervention. We report all depositor-level figures as placeholders pending completion of the on-chain event extraction pipeline, and we cross-reference a companion paper for on-chain implementation details.

## 2. Results

### 2.1 Framework validation: independent recovery of the CCP quality threshold

The quality framework assigns each credit a composite score (0--100) across six weighted dimensions: removal type hierarchy (0.250), additionality (0.200), measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV; 0.200), permanence (0.175), vintage year (0.100), and registry methodology quality (0.075). A seventh dimension, co-benefits, carries zero weight and serves only as a safeguards-gate disqualifier trigger (see Methods). Composites map to six letter grades: AAA (>=90), AA (>=75), A (>=60), BBB (>=45), BB (>=30), and B (<30).

To validate the framework without training on any external quality signal, we scored 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories and split the dataset by ICVCM Core Carbon Principles eligibility -- the most authoritative quality signal currently available in the VCM. The CCP label certifies that a methodology meets threshold requirements for additionality, permanence, and robust quantification; it is a binary quality gate applied at the methodology level by an independent governance body.

The framework produced a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible credits (*n* = 165, mean ordinal grade = 2.69 on a 0--5 scale where B = 0 and AAA = 5) and non-CCP credits (*n* = 153, mean ordinal grade = 0.70) (Fig. 1). No CCP-eligible credit scored B; no non-CCP credit scored A or AAA. The modal grade for CCP-eligible credits was BBB (43%); for non-CCP credits, B (54%). Cohen's *d* = 1.80 (95% bootstrap CI: 1.50--2.16, 10,000 resamples), common language effect size = 91.4% (CI: 87.5--94.8%), and Cliff's delta = 0.83 (CI: 0.75--0.90) all indicate near-complete separation.

This gap constitutes a recovery of a threshold measured through a separate governance process. Calyx Global, an ICVCM-appointed rating agency, reported that CCP-eligible projects average approximately two grade levels above non-CCP projects on their proprietary scale. Our gap of 1.99 grade levels, derived from a separately constructed framework with different dimension definitions and weights, converges on the same magnitude without any calibration to the CCP label. We note that the framework's weight allocation draws partly on the same integrity principles that inform CCP assessment criteria (additionality, permanence, MRV). The CCP calibration therefore represents partial convergent validation rather than fully independent external validation. If the framework's weights were miscalibrated -- systematically overweighting or underweighting the dimensions that CCP selects for -- the separation would be either implausibly wide or implausibly narrow. Convergence under partially shared assumptions constitutes evidence that the weight vector captures the quality construct that CCP was designed to certify.

### 2.2 Framework correlates with agencies better than they correlate with each other

We next assessed whether the framework produces credit-level rankings consistent with commercial rating agencies. Using the expanded dataset of 27 projects spanning REDD+, direct air capture, biochar, cookstoves, improved forest management, methane abatement, landfill gas, and renewable energy, we computed Spearman rank correlations between our framework and each agency with available public ratings.

The framework's correlation with BeZero Carbon was *rho* = +0.901 (*n* = 27, 95% bootstrap CI [.783, .959], permutation *p* < .0001) (Fig. 2). This strong agreement spans the full quality spectrum, from AAA (Climeworks Orca, exact match) through B (Chinese onshore wind, directional match). The mean framework-versus-agency correlation across all agencies with available pairwise data was +0.343, compared with a mean inter-agency correlation of +0.009. The framework's agreement with commercial raters materially exceeds the commercial raters' agreement with each other.

The inter-agency baseline merits emphasis. BeZero and Calyx Global were anti-correlated at *rho* = -0.664 on the six REDD+ projects where simultaneous public ratings exist -- a genuine directional disagreement on the same credits. This anti-correlation is concentrated in avoided-deforestation projects, where baseline counterfactuals are inherently uncertain. On engineered removals and industrial projects, both the framework and the agencies converge.

Subgroup analysis revealed instructive variation. On carbon dioxide removal projects (DACCS, biochar, enhanced weathering), the framework-versus-BeZero correlation reached *rho* = +0.973 -- near-perfect agreement on projects with clear quality signals. On avoidance projects (cookstoves, landfill gas, renewable energy), the correlation was *rho* = +0.802, reflecting a systematic and documented design choice: the framework applies the Oxford Principles hierarchy, which scores avoidance credits below removal credits regardless of co-benefit narratives, producing a BBB ceiling for cookstoves that commercial agencies rate higher. This divergence is transparent, deliberate, and re-weightable by users who adopt different normative positions on the avoidance-removal hierarchy.

### 2.3 Quality atlas reveals 10-fold variation across the VCM

Having established the framework's validity, we computed the Pool Quality Deficit (PQD) across 34 methodology-vintage segments spanning the entire voluntary carbon market (Fig. 3). The PQD is defined as PQD = 1 - (*C-bar* / 100), where *C-bar* is the mean composite quality score of credits in a segment. A PQD approaching 1.0 indicates near-complete quality failure; a PQD approaching 0 indicates minimal quality degradation.

The quality atlas reveals dramatic variation. At one extreme, DACCS with geological storage achieves PQD = 0.076 (*n* = 14, mean composite = 92.4), representing the empirical floor for market-available credits. At the other, pre-2016 grid-connected renewable energy reaches PQD = 0.759 (*n* = 18), reflecting the ICVCM's rejection of additionality claims for grid-scale renewables in markets where they are already cost-competitive. The 10-fold range between these extremes -- 0.076 to 0.759 -- quantifies the heterogeneity that quality-blind pooling renders invisible.

To assess whether observed pool quality is attributable to adverse selection rather than random credit composition, we constructed a null model. Under random deposit from the 318-credit population, the expected PQD is 0.51 (SD = 0.032). BCT's observed PQD of 0.724 falls 6.2 standard deviations above this null, rejecting the hypothesis that BCT's quality profile reflects random credit selection (*p* < 10^-9). This is not a pool that happened to accumulate low-quality credits; it is a pool whose quality profile is consistent with systematic adverse selection. However, pool-level PQD alone cannot distinguish adverse selection from other mechanisms; depositor-level analysis (Results 2.4) is required to establish the strategic sorting mechanism.

CCP filtering narrows the quality gap but does not close it. Among CCP-eligible methodology segments only, the mean PQD is 0.419 -- a 37% reduction from BCT's 0.724 but still above the 0.51 null-model expectation for the full population. The residual quality deficit within CCP-eligible categories reflects within-methodology quality variation that the binary CCP label does not capture, particularly vintage effects: pre-2020 CCP-eligible credits average PQD = 0.687, while 2024+ vintages average 0.273. The vintage gradient confirms that temporal discounting is a first-order quality dimension that pooling mechanisms must address.

### 2.4 Depositor-level evidence of adverse selection

The PQD establishes that BCT's quality is far below what random selection would produce, but pool-level statistics alone cannot distinguish adverse selection (rational depositors strategically depositing low-quality credits) from other explanations (e.g., low-quality projects being disproportionately tokenized for reasons unrelated to depositor intent). ### 2.5 Supply-side corroboration: pool-level quality degradation

The depositor-level analysis establishes that individual market participants engage in strategic quality sorting. The pool-level PQD corroborates this finding from the supply side.

BCT at its 2022 peak inventory exhibited PQD = 0.724 (*n* = 43 credits, mean composite = 27.6, zero credits rated A or above). Every credit in the pool fell below the BBB threshold. Moss MCO2 performed similarly (PQD = 0.713, *n* = 30), with only 6.7% of credits reaching A grade. Both pools operated without any quality filter beyond basic registry eligibility and accumulated credits dominated by old-vintage REDD+, pre-2013 industrial gas destruction, and CDM-era renewable energy -- precisely the categories flagged as high-risk by Calel et al. and Trencher et al.

Toucan's CHAR pool provides a natural experiment (Fig. 3). CHAR restricts deposits to a narrow allowlist of biochar projects with durable storage, achieving PQD = 0.221 (*n* = 12, all credits rated A or above). CHAR and BCT operated on the same blockchain infrastructure and drew from overlapping registries, yet their quality profiles diverge by more than half the theoretical range of the PQD. The 0.503-point gap between BCT and CHAR is not attributable to market size or liquidity differences; it arises directly from CHAR's decision to restrict eligible credit types to a category that inherently scores well on removal type, permanence, and MRV.

This supply-side evidence converges with CarbonPlan's independent analysis of on-chain credit quality. CarbonPlan documented that tokenized carbon credit pools are dominated by credits from projects with documented integrity concerns -- a supply-side finding. Our depositor-level analysis adds the demand-side mechanism: it is not merely that low-quality credits ended up in pools, but that depositors *chose* to put them there while retaining their better credits. Supply-side quality degradation (CarbonPlan) plus depositor-side strategic sorting (this study) forms a pincer that closes the empirical case for Akerlof-type adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets.

### 2.6 Quality gating as market intervention

The preceding results establish that adverse selection in carbon pools is measurable and attributable to depositor behaviour. The practical question is whether market design can reverse it. We conducted counterfactual simulations on all six pools, progressively raising the minimum grade threshold from B (no gate) to AAA and computing the resulting PQD at each level (Fig. 7).

For BCT, a BBB-minimum gate reduced the PQD from 0.724 to 0.405 -- a 44% improvement -- while admitting only 2 of 43 credits (5%). No credit in BCT's 2022 inventory met the A threshold, meaning that any gate above BBB would have emptied the pool entirely. This result underscores the severity of BCT's quality degradation: the pool contained no credit that a moderately stringent quality filter would have accepted.

For the KlimaDAO kVCM inventory, the same BBB gate reduced the PQD from 0.519 to 0.273 while retaining 40% of credits (8 of 20). Unlike BCT, kVCM retains viable credits at every threshold -- evidence that curation at the pool-design level shifts the entire quality distribution upward. An AA gate further reduced the index to 0.181.

The key insight is that quality gating is a market mechanism, not a technology prescription. It does not require blockchain, smart contracts, or any specific infrastructure -- only that quality information be available at the point where credits enter a pooled instrument. Singapore's National Environment Agency (NEA) in 2025 became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit ratings for carbon tax offset eligibility, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as approved raters. This regulatory precedent demonstrates that the transition from voluntary to mandatory quality differentiation is already underway. The counterfactual simulations quantify what such quality gates would achieve: had BCT enforced a BBB gate at deposit time, 95% of its historical inventory would have been rejected, but the remaining 5% would have maintained a PQD below 0.5 rather than above 0.7 -- potentially averting the market confidence collapse that followed.

The CHAR pool's success demonstrates that quality gating need not be sophisticated to be effective. CHAR's biochar-only allowlist is a blunt binary instrument, yet it achieves PQD = 0.221 -- comparable to what a BBB gate achieves on KlimaDAO's curated inventory (PQD = 0.273). The framework we present generalises CHAR's binary curation to a continuous, parameterisable quality threshold applicable to any pool regardless of credit type composition. The 100% gate accuracy assumption in our simulations represents an upper bound; real-world implementation would need to contend with scoring uncertainty, and the framework's distributional scoring model (see Methods) provides the probabilistic machinery to do so.
