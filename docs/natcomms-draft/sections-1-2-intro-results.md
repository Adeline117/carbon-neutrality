# Quantifying adverse selection in tokenized carbon credit pools

## 1. Introduction

The voluntary carbon market (VCM) has a quality problem that economic theory predicted but no one has measured. Annual trading volume surpassed $2 billion in 2023, yet recent empirical work suggests that fewer than 16% of credits traded represent real emission reductions^1^. Corporate offset portfolios fare no better: 87% of credits retired by major firms carry high risk of non-additionality or over-crediting^2^. These findings raise a question that aggregate statistics alone cannot answer: why does a market that everyone acknowledges is failing continue to attract capital?

The answer lies in a mechanism that George Akerlof identified more than fifty years ago^3^. When buyers cannot distinguish high-quality goods from low-quality goods, sellers of low-quality goods capture a disproportionate share of the market. Prices converge on the average quality rather than the marginal quality, and high-quality producers exit because they cannot recover their costs. Akerlof called this adverse selection; in colloquial terms, the market is flooded with lemons.

Carbon credit pools accelerate this dynamic. Tokenized pool instruments such as Toucan's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) and Moss's MCO2 were designed to aggregate heterogeneous carbon credits into a single fungible token, enabling liquidity and DeFi composability^4^. In doing so, they explicitly treated all deposited credits as interchangeable. A direct air capture credit with geological storage and a decade-old avoided-deforestation credit with contested baselines received the same pool token at the same price. Rational depositors responded predictably: they deposited their lowest-quality eligible credits and retained their highest-quality ones. Manshadi et al.^5^ formalized this process, showing that quality-blind pooling produces a separating equilibrium in which high-integrity credits are systematically excluded.

The VCM has not lacked attempts at quality differentiation. Commercial rating agencies --- BeZero Carbon, Calyx Global, Sylvera, and MSCI --- now rate thousands of projects, and the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM) launched its Core Carbon Principles (CCP) label to certify methodology-level quality^6^. CCP-labelled credits command a roughly 25% price premium^7^, and high-integrity credits trade at up to four times the price of low-integrity credits^8^. Yet three structural gaps persist. First, commercial ratings are proprietary and show significant inter-agency disagreement --- on a six-project REDD+ overlap sample, the mean pairwise Spearman correlation among BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera was +0.009, effectively indistinguishable from random^9^. Second, no rating agency provides a callable on-chain interface, meaning that smart contracts cannot enforce quality gates at deposit time. Third, and most fundamentally, there exists no quantitative metric for the severity of adverse selection in a specific pool. We know that BCT suffered quality degradation; we do not know, in a comparable and reproducible way, how much.

Here we address all three gaps. We introduce the Lemons Index (LI), defined as the complement of the mean composite quality score normalized to the unit interval: $L = 1 - (\bar{c} / 100)$, where $\bar{c}$ is the mean composite score of all credits in a pool. A Lemons Index of 0 indicates a pool in which every credit scores perfectly; a Lemons Index of 1 indicates a pool in which every credit scores zero. The metric is interpretable, pool-comparable, and computable from publicly available project data.

We validate the Lemons Index against a dataset of 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories, scored on a seven-dimension quality framework that we calibrate against the ICVCM CCP label (the VCM's closest approximation to ground truth) and against public commercial agency ratings. We present four principal contributions. First, we compute the Lemons Index for six real and hypothetical carbon credit pools, revealing a spectrum of adverse selection severity from 0.724 (Toucan BCT at its 2022 peak) to 0.100 (a hypothetical pool restricted to the highest-rated credits). Second, we demonstrate that our quality framework recovers the CCP quality threshold with a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits (Cohen's $d$ = 1.80, common language effect size = 91.4%), consistent with independently published measurements by Calyx Global^10^. Third, we show that automated quality gating --- implemented as a callable `meetsGrade()` interface on Ethereum --- reverses adverse selection, reducing BCT's Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 under a BBB minimum gate. Fourth, we validate rating reproducibility through an independent multi-rater panel (Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600, ICC = 0.993) and Monte Carlo weight sensitivity analysis (93.7% global grade robustness across 10,000 Dirichlet-sampled weight vectors).

Taken together, these results establish that adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets is measurable, severe, and reversible through on-chain quality infrastructure.


## 2. Results

### Adverse selection severity varies dramatically across carbon credit pools

We define the Lemons Index as $L = 1 - (\bar{c} / 100)$, where $\bar{c}$ is the mean composite quality score across all credits in a pool, computed from seven weighted dimensions (see Methods). A Lemons Index approaching 1.0 indicates that the pool's average credit quality is near zero --- a market dominated by lemons. A Lemons Index approaching 0 indicates that adverse selection has been effectively eliminated.

We computed the Lemons Index for six pools spanning the full range of existing on-chain carbon credit instruments (Fig. 1). Toucan BCT at its 2022 peak inventory of 43 credits exhibited a Lemons Index of 0.724, with a mean composite score of 27.6 out of 100 and zero credits rated A or above. Every credit in the pool fell below the BBB threshold that we subsequently identify as the minimum viable quality gate. Moss MCO2 performed similarly (LI = 0.713, $n$ = 30), with only 6.7% of credits reaching A grade or above. Both pools operated without any quality filter beyond basic registry eligibility, and both accumulated credits dominated by old-vintage REDD+ projects, pre-2013 industrial gas destruction, and CDM-era renewable energy --- precisely the credit categories that systematic evaluations have flagged as high risk for non-additionality and over-crediting^1,2^.

The remaining pools trace a monotonic decline in adverse selection severity that tracks the stringency of their quality controls. Toucan NCT (LI = 0.601, $n$ = 28), which restricts deposits to nature-based credits, showed moderate improvement. KlimaDAO's 2.0 kVCM inventory (LI = 0.519, $n$ = 20), which applies a curated allowlist on Base, reduced the index further. Toucan CHAR (LI = 0.221, $n$ = 12), a biochar-specific pool on Base that restricts deposits to a narrow allowlist of high-integrity biochar projects, achieved a Lemons Index less than one-third of BCT's. All 12 credits in CHAR scored A or above. A hypothetical pool restricted to credits rated AAA under our framework (LI = 0.100, $n$ = 13) represents the empirical floor: even among the highest-quality credits, vintage variation and per-project score adjustments prevent mean composites from reaching 100.

The 0.503-point gap between BCT (0.724) and CHAR (0.221) quantifies the value of quality curation. BCT and CHAR operated on the same blockchain infrastructure and drew from overlapping registries, yet their quality profiles diverge by more than half the theoretical range of the index. This divergence is not attributable to market size or liquidity differences; it arises directly from CHAR's decision to restrict eligible credit types to a category --- biochar with durable storage --- that scores consistently well on removal type, permanence, and MRV dimensions.

### A seven-dimension quality framework recovers the ICVCM quality threshold

The Lemons Index depends on an underlying quality score. We developed a seven-dimension composite framework (see Methods for full specification) that scores each credit on removal type (weight 0.25), additionality (0.20), measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV; 0.20), permanence (0.175), vintage year (0.10), co-benefits (0, safeguards-gate only), and registry methodology (0.075). Seven disqualifiers --- including double counting, sanctioned registry, and community harm --- impose grade caps that override the composite score when triggered.

To validate the framework's weight calibration without expert consultation, we scored 318 credits spanning 17 methodology categories and split the dataset by ICVCM CCP eligibility --- the most authoritative quality signal currently available in the VCM^6^. The CCP label certifies that a methodology meets threshold requirements for additionality, permanence, and robust quantification; it represents a binary quality gate applied at the methodology level by an independent governance body.

The framework produced a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible credits ($n$ = 165, mean ordinal grade = 2.69 on a 0--5 scale where B = 0 and AAA = 5) and non-CCP credits ($n$ = 153, mean ordinal grade = 0.70) (Fig. 2). No CCP-eligible credit scored B; no non-CCP credit scored A or AAA. The modal grade for CCP-eligible credits was BBB (43%); for non-CCP credits it was B (54%). The overlap between the two distributions was confined to BB (11% of CCP, 39% of non-CCP) and AA (17% of CCP, 8% of non-CCP), reflecting the heterogeneity within each category rather than a failure of separation.

The effect size was large by conventional standards. Cohen's $d$ = 1.80 (95% CI: 1.50--2.16), computed with pooled standard deviations from 10,000 bootstrap resamples, places the CCP/non-CCP separation well above the threshold ($d$ > 0.8) for a large effect^11^. The common language effect size (CLES) was 91.4% (95% CI: 87.5--94.8%): a randomly chosen CCP-eligible credit outscored a randomly chosen non-CCP credit in more than nine out of ten pairings. A Mann-Whitney $U$ test confirmed that the distributions were significantly different ($z$ = 13.06, $p$ $\approx$ 0). Cliff's delta = 0.83 (95% CI: 0.75--0.90) corroborated the near-complete separation.

This gap is independently validated. Calyx Global, an ICVCM-appointed rating agency, reported that CCP-eligible projects average approximately two grade levels above non-CCP projects on their proprietary scale^10^. Our gap of 1.99 grade levels, derived from an independently constructed framework with different dimension definitions and weights, converges on the same magnitude. If our weights were miscalibrated --- overweighting or underweighting the dimensions that CCP selects for --- the separation would be either implausibly wide or implausibly narrow. Convergence with an independent measurement constitutes evidence that the weight vector is well calibrated at the population level.

### Framework agreement exceeds inter-agency agreement

We next assessed whether the framework's credit-level rankings are consistent with those of commercial rating agencies. Using the only publicly available multi-rater overlap dataset --- six REDD+ projects rated by BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera, compiled by Carbon Market Watch^9^ --- we computed pairwise Spearman rank correlations with our framework as a fourth rater.

On this REDD+-only sample ($n$ = 6), the mean Spearman correlation between our framework and commercial agencies was $\rho$ = +0.343, compared with a mean inter-agency correlation of $\rho$ = +0.009 (Fig. 3). The inter-agency baseline is effectively zero: BeZero and Calyx Global were strongly anti-correlated ($\rho$ = -0.664) on the same six projects, reflecting systematic disagreement on how to weight additionality concerns in avoided-deforestation projects. Our framework's strongest pairwise agreement was with BeZero ($\rho$ = +0.664) and Sylvera ($\rho$ = +0.566).

When expanded to a cross-methodology dataset that includes direct air capture, biochar, industrial avoidance, and renewable energy credits alongside REDD+ ($n$ = 15), the correlation with BeZero rose to $\rho$ = +0.913 ($p$ < 0.001, permutation test with 10,000 iterations). This strong agreement on the expanded dataset, contrasted with moderate agreement on REDD+ alone, is consistent with a known pattern: inter-agency disagreement is concentrated in REDD+ and nature-based credits, where baseline counterfactuals are inherently uncertain, while engineered removal and industrial avoidance credits generate broader consensus^9^.

One systematic divergence warrants disclosure. Our framework ranks cookstove credits lower than some commercial agencies do, because the removal-type dimension applies the Oxford Principles hierarchy^12^, which scores avoidance credits below removal credits regardless of co-benefit narratives. Berg et al.^13^ documented that co-benefit narratives can inflate perceived quality beyond what carbon integrity warrants. Our safeguards-gate design --- which removes co-benefits from the composite and uses them only as a disqualifier trigger --- represents a normative choice that we regard as a strength: it prevents narrative washing from inflating composite scores. However, it produces systematic divergence from agencies that weight co-benefits positively.

### Quality ratings are reproducible across independent raters

A quality framework is useful only if independent raters applying the same rubric reach the same conclusions. We tested inter-rater reliability using a panel of three Claude-family language models (Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, Haiku 4.5), each scoring 29 credits independently in isolated sessions using redacted evidence packs with author grades removed (see Methods).

Grade-level Fleiss' $\kappa$ across the three-rater panel was 0.600, at the boundary of Landis and Koch's^14^ "substantial" agreement threshold ($\kappa$ $\geq$ 0.61). Composite-level intraclass correlation (ICC(2,$k$)) was 0.993, indicating near-perfect reliability on the continuous outcome. The author's grades matched the panel median exactly on 25 of 29 credits (86%), and every discrepancy was confined to a single adjacent grade band (100% within $\pm$1).

Per-dimension reliability varied substantially (Table 1). Permanence ($\kappa$ = 0.684) and removal type ($\kappa$ = 0.585) --- the two dimensions that carry the most weight and most strongly determine whether a credit reaches the top grades --- showed the strongest agreement. These are the dimensions where the rubric provides the crispest categorical distinctions (engineered vs. nature-based removal; geological vs. biological storage). Additionality ($\kappa$ = 0.243) and MRV ($\kappa$ = 0.248) showed fair agreement, consistent with the inherent subjectivity in assessing counterfactual baselines and verification quality from project documentation. Registry methodology ($\kappa$ = 0.168) showed the weakest agreement, driven by ambiguity in the original four-tier scoring rubric --- a finding that motivated a simplification to a two-tier scheme (CCP-eligible vs. non-CCP) in the current framework version. Co-benefits ($\kappa$ = 0.182) also showed slight agreement, but this dimension carries zero weight in the composite and serves only as a disqualifier trigger; its low $\kappa$ does not propagate into grade-level disagreement.

The high composite ICC despite moderate per-dimension $\kappa$ values is a designed property of the framework: the weighted composite mean smooths out per-dimension noise, producing stable grades even when individual dimension scores diverge by 5--12 points.

We further tested the framework's robustness to adversarial manipulation using five purpose-built attack credits, each targeting a specific potential vulnerability: narrative washing through co-benefit inflation, double counting of a technically perfect credit, registry shopping via a prestigious registry with a rejected methodology, vintage arbitrage through re-tokenization of old credits, and biodiversity destruction by a monoculture plantation maximizing carbon uptake^15^. All five attacks were correctly identified: the framework assigned the expected grade in every case, with disqualifiers and safeguards-gate mechanisms preventing score inflation. The narrative-washing cookstove (ADV01) scored BB rather than the BBB--A range it would have reached under a co-benefits-weighted scheme. The double-counted credit (ADV02) was capped at B despite AAA-level technical scores.

### Quality gating reverses adverse selection

The preceding results establish that adverse selection in carbon pools is measurable and that a validated quality framework exists. The practical question is whether quality gating can reverse it. We conducted counterfactual simulations on all six pools, progressively raising the minimum grade threshold from B (no gate) to AAA and computing the resulting Lemons Index at each level (Fig. 4).

For BCT, a minimum gate at BBB reduced the Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 --- a 44% improvement --- while admitting only 2 of 43 credits (5%). A gate at BB produced an intermediate improvement (LI = 0.547, 7 credits admitted). No credits in BCT's 2022 inventory met the A threshold, meaning that any gate above BBB would have emptied the pool entirely. This result underscores the severity of BCT's adverse selection: the pool contained no credit that a moderately stringent quality filter would have accepted.

CHAR provides a natural experiment. Its biochar-only allowlist admits all 12 credits at every gate threshold up to and including AA, producing an unchanged Lemons Index of 0.221 regardless of the gate level. CHAR achieves through category restriction what a generic quality gate would need to enforce through per-credit scoring: by admitting only a credit type that inherently scores well on removal type (biochar: 75--84), permanence (>100 year storage commitments), and MRV (third-party verified biochar quantification), the pool avoids adverse selection without requiring an on-chain quality oracle.

The KlimaDAO kVCM inventory illustrates an intermediate case. Its curated allowlist already excludes the worst credits, producing a baseline LI of 0.519. A BBB gate reduces this to 0.273, admitting 8 of 20 credits, all rated A or above. An AA gate further reduces the index to 0.181. Unlike BCT, kVCM retains viable credits at every threshold --- evidence that curation at the pool-design level shifts the entire quality distribution upward.

To make quality gating enforceable without trusted intermediaries, we implemented the `meetsGrade()` function as a Solidity view function conforming to the ERC-CCQR interface standard (see Methods). The function is callable at zero marginal gas cost by any deposit contract, retirement gate, or DeFi protocol. A pool contract that calls `meetsGrade(creditAddress, tokenId, Grade.BBB)` before accepting a deposit would have prevented 95% of BCT's historical inventory from entering the pool. This is not a hypothetical: the CHAR pool's `checkEligible()` function already implements binary quality gating on Base, and the `meetsGrade()` interface generalizes this to continuous quality thresholds.

### Rating stability under weight perturbation

A composite score is only as credible as its sensitivity to the specific weight vector chosen. We assessed robustness through Monte Carlo simulation, drawing 10,000 weight vectors from a Dirichlet distribution centred on the framework's weights with a concentration parameter of 50 (see Methods). For each draw, we recomputed all 29 pilot-credit composites and recorded whether each credit's grade changed.

Global robustness --- the mean proportion of weight draws under which a credit's grade remains unchanged --- was 93.7% (Table 2). Twenty-four of 29 credits maintained their grade in more than 90% of draws. Five credits were flagged as fragile (stability < 90%): Plan Vivo agroforestry (51.5% stability, composite 60.15, on the A/BBB boundary), Gold Standard cookstoves (66.1%, composite 46.33, on the BBB/BB boundary), Charm Industrial bio-oil injection (72.7%, composite 90.53, on the AAA/AA boundary), Pachama-verified reforestation (74.0%, composite 74.05, on the A/AA boundary), and adipic acid N$_2$O destruction (78.1%, composite 57.27, on the BBB/A boundary). All five fragile credits sit within 3 points of a grade boundary, confirming that instability is a mechanical consequence of boundary proximity rather than a systemic framework deficiency. At the extremes --- Climeworks Orca (AAA, 100% stable), Kariba REDD+ (B, 100% stable), and all four synthetic disqualifier credits (100% stable) --- the framework produces invariant grades regardless of weight perturbation.

At higher concentration (tighter prior, $\alpha$ = 100), global robustness rose to 95.4% with the same five fragile credits. At lower concentration (wider prior, $\alpha$ = 20), robustness fell to 90.1% with 10 fragile credits. The monotonic relationship between concentration and robustness confirms that the framework degrades gracefully: even under substantial weight uncertainty, more than nine in ten credits retain their baseline grade.

Cross-temporal stability provides a complementary robustness check. Between framework versions v0.3 and v0.4 --- which changed four of seven dimension weights, introduced the safeguards-gate, and added a disqualifier --- 24 of 29 credits (82.8%) retained their grade, and all changes were confined to a single grade band. Between v0.4 and v0.6 --- which collapsed the registry dimension from four tiers to two and tightened the vintage formula --- all 29 credits (100%) retained their grade. The Spearman rank correlation between v0.3 and v0.6 composite scores was $\rho$ = 0.992, indicating that the relative ordering of credits is preserved across framework iterations. The decreasing magnitude of cross-version perturbation (5 grade changes in v0.3 to v0.4, 0 in v0.4 to v0.6) provides evidence of convergence: the framework is stabilizing as rubric ambiguities are resolved.
