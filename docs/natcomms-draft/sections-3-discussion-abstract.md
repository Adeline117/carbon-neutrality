# Discussion and Abstract --- Nature Communications Draft

**Paper**: "Anatomy of a market failure: on-chain evidence reveals renewable energy credits, not REDD+, drove the collapse of tokenized carbon markets"

## Discussion

### The REDD+ narrative was wrong

The most consequential finding in this study is compositional: Toucan BCT, the largest and most scrutinised tokenized carbon credit pool, was 74.1% renewable energy credits and 1.5% REDD+. The prevailing academic and industry narrative --- that BCT collapsed because of low-quality REDD+ projects with contested baselines --- is factually incorrect with respect to the pool's actual on-chain composition. This matters because the narrative has shaped policy: pool designers have focused on excluding nature-based credits, regulators have prioritised REDD+ oversight, and the broader VCM discourse has treated REDD+ as the primary quality problem. Our data show that the primary quality problem was elsewhere entirely.

The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial --- high-profile investigations of Kariba, Cordillera Azul, and Amazon basin projects generated extensive media coverage and academic scrutiny^1,2,3^ --- while the quality problems of CDM-era renewable energy credits are structurally invisible. A Chinese wind farm registered in 2010 does not generate controversy: the wind farm exists, the electricity is real, the monitoring data are accurate. The quality failure is not in the project's execution but in its additionality: the wind farm would have been built regardless of carbon revenue, because grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010. This form of quality failure --- a project that delivers real outputs but no additional emission reductions --- is harder to detect, less newsworthy, and more prevalent than the baseline manipulation problems that dominate the REDD+ discourse.

The implication for policy is direct: quality infrastructure must address the additionality dimension with the same rigour currently applied to baseline accuracy. The ICVCM's decision not to grant CCP eligibility to grid-connected renewable energy credits is consistent with our findings, but the VCM still contains billions of tonnes of legacy renewable energy credits that can be deposited into any pool that does not actively exclude them. BCT's composition demonstrates what happens when they are not excluded.

### Renewable energy credits are the hidden quality crisis

Our 34-segment quality atlas positions grid-connected renewables at the bottom of the quality spectrum (PQD = 0.759), below even the contested REDD+ categories (PQD 0.690--0.720). This finding has not been previously documented at the credit level, though it is consistent with aggregate assessments: Calel et al.^4^ estimated that fewer than 16% of credits represent real emission reductions, and the bulk of non-additional credits in their sample were avoidance-based, including renewables. Trencher et al.^5^ found that 87% of corporate offset purchases carry high risk of non-additionality, with renewable energy credits accounting for a substantial share.

The quality atlas reveals that the VCM's quality problem is not uniform: it is concentrated in specific segments (renewables, industrial gas destruction, pre-2013 avoidance credits) while other segments (engineered CDR, biochar, recent cookstoves with digital MRV) exhibit quality profiles that could support functional markets. The 10-fold PQD range from 0.076 to 0.759 means that treating the VCM as a single market with a single quality level is misleading. There are multiple quality markets operating under a single label.

### Akerlof's endstate: once reached, recovery requires quality infrastructure

BCT's PQD of 0.685 with 100% of credits at BB or B represents the theoretical endstate of Akerlof's adverse selection model: a market in which high-quality goods have been completely driven out and only lemons remain. The price collapse from $7 to $0.50 confirms the theoretical prediction that market price converges on the reservation value of the lowest-quality supply. This is, to our knowledge, the first empirical observation of Akerlof's complete quality collapse in a real market with credit-level quality data.

The significance of reaching the endstate is that recovery requires a discontinuous intervention. In Akerlof's framework, once the market has collapsed, incremental improvements in average quality are insufficient: buyers have exited, trust has been destroyed, and the market-clearing price is too low to attract high-quality supply. Recovery requires the introduction of a credible quality signal --- what Akerlof called "counteracting institutions" --- that enables buyers to distinguish high-quality from low-quality goods before purchase.

In the carbon market context, quality gating serves as this counteracting institution. The 0.460-point PQD gap between BCT (0.685) and CHAR (0.221) quantifies the effect of one form of quality gating (category restriction). Our counterfactual simulations show that a continuous quality gate at the BBB threshold would have reduced BCT's PQD from 0.685 to 0.405, and a gate at BB would have achieved 0.547. The Klima 2.0 kVCM inventory on Base, which applies curated selection, achieves a PQD of 0.519 without explicit quality scoring --- demonstrating that even informal curation shifts the quality distribution away from the Akerlof endstate.

The practical implication is that market recovery requires not just better credits but better quality infrastructure: systems that make quality observable, comparable, and enforceable at the point of transaction. Singapore's 2025 mandate for commercial carbon credit ratings^6^ represents the first sovereign step in this direction; the on-chain implementation described in our companion paper^7^ provides the technical mechanism for enforcement.

### Agency disagreement: the quality information market has failed

The BeZero--Calyx anti-correlation of $\rho$ = -0.664 on the six-project REDD+ overlap sample is not merely a statistical curiosity. It means that two major carbon credit rating agencies, assessing the same projects using the same public information, produce rankings that are worse than random: an investor following BeZero's recommendations would systematically select the projects that Calyx ranks lowest, and vice versa. The mean inter-agency Spearman of +0.009 across all three agency pairs (BeZero--Calyx, BeZero--Sylvera, Calyx--Sylvera) is effectively indistinguishable from zero.

This finding is consistent with Carbon Market Watch's qualitative assessment^8^ that rating agencies "rate similar projects very differently," but our quantification is more severe than the qualitative description implies. An inter-agency correlation of +0.009 means that commercial ratings provide essentially no shared signal about relative project quality on the REDD+ projects where quality assessment matters most. The quality information market itself has failed: the institutions designed to resolve information asymmetry have not converged on a shared understanding of quality.

Two factors contribute to the disagreement. First, REDD+ credit quality depends heavily on counterfactual baselines --- what would have happened to the forest without the project --- and different agencies apply different assumptions about baseline deforestation rates, leakage, and permanence. Second, agencies weight quality dimensions differently: Calyx applies stringent cross-sectoral standards that penalise REDD+ projects relative to engineered removals, while BeZero treats additionality as a "limiting factor" rather than a strict cap.

Our framework's higher mean agreement with agencies ($\rho$ = +0.343 vs. +0.009) should not be interpreted as evidence that our framework is "more correct" than commercial agencies. Rather, it reflects a structural property of our design: by using explicit, documented weights and a transparent composite formula, the framework avoids the systematic divergences that arise when agencies apply implicit and potentially inconsistent methodological choices. On the cross-methodology dataset ($n$ = 15), where quality signals are clearer (engineered CDR, industrial avoidance), our framework converges with BeZero at $\rho$ = +0.901, consistent with the observation that inter-agency disagreement is concentrated in credit categories where the underlying science is disputed.

### Open, reproducible quality infrastructure as prerequisite for market recovery

The combination of composition misattribution, complete quality collapse, and agency disagreement points to a single structural diagnosis: the VCM lacks transparent, reproducible quality infrastructure. Commercial ratings are proprietary and, on REDD+ projects, mutually contradictory. The CCP label is binary (pass/fail) and does not differentiate quality within the CCP-eligible universe (where PQD still varies from 0.076 to 0.419). No existing quality system provides a callable interface that smart contracts can use to enforce quality gates at the point of deposit or retirement.

Our framework addresses this gap through three design choices. First, openness: all rubrics are machine-readable JSON, the scoring engine is open-source, and the full dataset is published, enabling any researcher to substitute alternative weights and recompute every result in this paper. Second, continuous quality differentiation: six-tier grades (AAA through B) and the continuous PQD metric distinguish quality within and across the CCP-eligible universe, not merely at the CCP boundary. Third, composability: the on-chain implementation (ERC-CCQR standard, detailed in our companion paper^7^) exposes a `meetsGrade()` function callable by any smart contract at zero marginal gas cost, enabling quality gating to be embedded in pool design, retirement flows, and DeFi protocols without requiring trusted intermediaries.

### Limitations

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, the quality scores are author-derived: although validated against CCP labels ($d$ = 1.80), commercial agency ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901), and an independent LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.600, ICC = 0.993), the per-dimension rubric reflects a single research group's judgment informed by literature synthesis rather than by structured expert elicitation. The framework's architecture is designed for iterative calibration: we have identified 20 domain experts for a Best-Worst Scaling consultation to produce empirically grounded weight priors.

Second, the inter-rater reliability study uses three Claude-family language models (Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, Haiku 4.5) rather than human domain experts. All three models share training data and alignment procedures, which may produce correlated biases invisible to within-family comparisons. A multi-provider replication incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and an open-weight model is needed to establish that the rubric's reproducibility extends beyond a single model family.

Third, the rank correlation with commercial agencies rests on modest samples ($n$ = 6 for the REDD+-only dataset, $n$ = 15 for the cross-methodology dataset). Expansion to 30 or more credits with publicly documented agency ratings would narrow confidence intervals and test generalisability across underrepresented project types such as enhanced weathering and ocean alkalinity enhancement.

Fourth, the BCT composition analysis relies on Verra registry metadata for project classification. Methodology categories are assigned by Verra at the project level; we did not independently verify the classification of each of the 90 projects. Misclassification at the registry level would propagate into our composition breakdown, though the dominance of renewable energy (74.1%) is large enough that even substantial reclassification would not change the qualitative finding.

Fifth, the PQD metric, like the composite score on which it is based, embeds normative assumptions: the Oxford Principles hierarchy (removal > avoidance), co-benefits excluded from the composite, and specific dimension weights. Users who disagree with these assumptions can re-weight dimensions and recompute all results using the published rubrics and scoring engine.

Sixth, the on-chain analysis covers BCT deposits on Polygon only. We did not analyse MCO2 (Moss) or NCT (Toucan nature-based) deposit transactions at the same level of granularity, though our pool-level PQD estimates for these pools (MCO2: 0.713, NCT: 0.601) are based on their documented compositions and are consistent with the BCT findings.

## Abstract

The collapse of Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- from $7 to $0.50 --- is widely attributed to low-quality REDD+ credits flooding the market. We analyse 547 on-chain deposit transactions (90 VCS projects, 7.5 million tonnes) on the Polygon blockchain and find this narrative is wrong. BCT was 74.1% renewable energy credits (grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar from China and India) and just 1.5% REDD+. The quality crisis was driven by CDM-era renewables with near-zero additionality, not by avoided-deforestation projects with contested baselines. Using an open, seven-dimension quality framework validated against the ICVCM Core Carbon Principles label (Cohen's $d$ = 1.80, 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits), BeZero Carbon ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901, $n$ = 15, 95% CI [0.783, 0.959]), and an independent rater panel (Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600, ICC = 0.993), we compute the Pool Quality Deficit for BCT at 0.685, with 63.3% of credits at grade B, 31.4% at BB, and 5.2% at BBB --- the complete quality collapse predicted by Akerlof's adverse selection model. A 34-segment quality atlas of the voluntary carbon market reveals 10-fold PQD variation (0.076 for direct air capture to 0.759 for grid-connected renewables), with BCT's composition sitting 6.2 standard deviations above the null model. Quality gating reverses the collapse: the Toucan CHAR biochar pool achieves PQD = 0.221 through category restriction, and counterfactual simulations show that a BBB minimum gate would have excluded 98% of BCT's credits. Meanwhile, the quality information infrastructure itself is broken: BeZero and Calyx Global anti-correlate at $\rho$ = -0.664 on the same projects, and the mean inter-agency agreement (+0.009) is indistinguishable from random. These findings demonstrate that transparent, reproducible quality measurement --- not proprietary ratings --- is the prerequisite for carbon market recovery.
