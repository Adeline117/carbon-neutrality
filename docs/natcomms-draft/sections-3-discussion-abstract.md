# Discussion and Abstract --- Nature Communications Draft

**Paper**: "Anatomy of a market failure: on-chain evidence reveals renewable energy credits, not REDD+, drove the collapse of tokenized carbon markets"

---

## Discussion

### The REDD+ narrative was wrong

The most consequential finding in this study is compositional: Toucan BCT, the largest and most scrutinised tokenized carbon credit pool, was 69.1% renewable energy credits and 4.2% REDD+. The prevailing academic and industry narrative --- that BCT collapsed because of low-quality REDD+ projects with contested baselines --- is factually incorrect with respect to the pool's actual on-chain composition. This matters because the narrative has shaped policy: pool designers have focused on excluding nature-based credits, regulators have prioritised REDD+ oversight, and the broader VCM discourse has treated REDD+ as the primary quality problem. Our data show that the primary quality problem was elsewhere entirely.

The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial --- high-profile investigations of Kariba, Cordillera Azul, and Amazon basin projects generated extensive media coverage and academic scrutiny^1,2,3^ --- while the quality problems of CDM-era renewable energy credits are structurally invisible. A Chinese wind farm registered in 2010 does not generate controversy: the wind farm exists, the electricity is real, the monitoring data are accurate. The quality failure is not in the project's execution but in its additionality: the wind farm would have been built regardless of carbon revenue, because grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010. This form of quality failure --- a project that delivers real outputs but no additional emission reductions --- is harder to detect, less newsworthy, and more prevalent than the baseline manipulation problems that dominate the REDD+ discourse.

The implication for policy is direct: quality infrastructure must address the additionality dimension with the same rigour currently applied to baseline accuracy. The ICVCM's decision not to grant CCP eligibility to grid-connected renewable energy credits is consistent with our findings, but the VCM still contains billions of tonnes of legacy renewable energy credits that can be deposited into any pool that does not actively exclude them. BCT's composition demonstrates what happens when they are not excluded.

### Renewable energy credits are the hidden quality crisis

Our 34-segment quality atlas positions grid-connected renewables at the bottom of the quality spectrum (PQD = 0.759), below even the contested REDD+ categories (PQD 0.690--0.720). This finding has not been previously documented at the credit level, though it is consistent with aggregate assessments: Calel et al.^4^ estimated that fewer than 16% of credits represent real emission reductions, and the bulk of non-additional credits in their sample were avoidance-based, including renewables. Trencher et al.^5^ found that 87% of corporate offset purchases carry high risk of non-additionality, with renewable energy credits accounting for a substantial share.

The quality atlas reveals that the VCM's quality problem is not uniform: it is concentrated in specific segments (renewables, industrial gas destruction, pre-2013 avoidance credits) while other segments (engineered CDR, biochar, recent cookstoves with digital MRV) exhibit quality profiles that could support functional markets. The 10-fold PQD range from 0.076 to 0.759 means that treating the VCM as a single market with a single quality level is misleading. There are multiple quality markets operating under a single label.

### Low-type pooling equilibrium: recovery requires quality infrastructure

BCT's PQD of 0.679 with 89.8% of tonnage at BB or B represents a low-type pooling equilibrium consistent with Gresham's law applied to uniform-price pools: low-quality credits drive out high-quality ones when the market fails to differentiate. The price collapse from $7 to $0.50 confirms that the market ultimately priced BCT at the reservation value of its dominant supply --- CDM-era renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality. BCT continued to trade (ruling out Akerlof's strict "no trade" endstate), but at a price that could only attract the lowest-quality credits, producing the pooling equilibrium we observe.

### Gresham dynamics and the limits of causal identification

The base-rate comparison is the paper's strongest empirical test. BCT's renewable share (69.1%) exceeds the VCS base rate ($\sim$37%) by a factor of 1.87 ($p$ < 10$^{-154}$), establishing quantitative over-selection. This finding does not require causal identification via an external control pool; it relies only on a comparison between BCT's composition and the universe of credits eligible for deposit. The over-selection is consistent with Gresham dynamics: CDM-era renewables were the cheapest credits available for bridging, and BCT's uniform pricing made them the most profitable to deposit.

We acknowledge several identification limitations. First, the BCT--NCT cross-pool comparison, while descriptively informative, cannot support causal inference. The two pools share 88.6% of their underlying tokens, have overlapping depositors (19 wallets), and NCT was designed after BCT's problems were observed. Cluster-robust bootstrap inference (clustering on TCO2 token) increases the standard error on the Spearman correlation difference by a factor of four, rendering the naive Fisher $z$-test ($p$ = 2.3 $\times$ 10$^{-7}$) non-significant under correct inference ($p$ = 0.24). No independent control pool with temporal data operated during BCT's lifetime: Toucan's monopoly on permissionless carbon tokenization in 2021--2022 precludes rigorous causal identification.

Second, the temporal quality decline ($\rho$ = $-$0.24 on the full sample) is primarily a vintage-composition effect. A robustness check using a vintage-free composite shows that the temporal correlation reverses to positive ($\rho$ = +0.24), indicating that the decline operates through progressively older vintages entering the pool, not through within-vintage quality deterioration. We cannot distinguish whether this vintage drift reflects strategic dumping of debunked credits (a Gresham mechanism) or supply exhaustion (older credits becoming the only remaining tokenisable inventory). Both mechanisms produce the same observable pattern.

Third, the compositional shift toward renewables is clear (Q1 67% $\rightarrow$ Q4 99.5%) but its causal attribution is ambiguous. Higher-quality types ceased depositing earlier, but this could reflect differential bridgeable supply, differential off-chain demand, or strategic exit. We report the exit pattern descriptively and note the identification gap.

What this study can establish is the *composition* and *selection coefficient* of the pool's quality failure, validated against external base rates. What it cannot establish is the precise behavioural mechanism driving that selection. The Gresham framing --- uniform pricing over observable heterogeneous quality drives out high-value goods --- is more appropriate than the Akerlof framing (which requires asymmetric information that was not present, since Verra metadata was public).

### Recovery requires quality infrastructure

The significance of reaching a low-type pooling equilibrium is that recovery requires a discontinuous intervention. Once a pool is dominated by low-quality supply, incremental improvements in average quality are insufficient: buyers have exited, trust has been destroyed, and the market-clearing price is too low to attract high-quality supply. Recovery requires the introduction of a credible quality differentiation mechanism that enables the market to price quality at the point of transaction.

In the carbon market context, quality gating serves as this mechanism. Pools that restrict deposits by credit type or allowlist exhibit descriptively better quality outcomes: BCT (no gate, PQD 0.679), NCT (type filter, PQD 0.601), CHAR (allowlist, PQD 0.221). We note that this gradient is observational, and the BCT--NCT comparison is confounded by extensive token overlap (88.6%) and endogenous pool design. Nevertheless, our counterfactual simulations show that a continuous quality gate at the BBB threshold would have reduced BCT's PQD from 0.679 to 0.405, and a gate at BB would have achieved 0.547. The Klima 2.0 kVCM inventory on Base, which applies curated selection, achieves a PQD of 0.519 without explicit quality scoring --- demonstrating that even informal curation shifts the quality distribution toward higher quality.

The practical implication is that market recovery requires not just better credits but better quality infrastructure: systems that make quality observable, comparable, and enforceable at the point of transaction. Singapore's 2025 mandate for commercial carbon credit ratings^6^ represents the first sovereign step in this direction; the on-chain implementation described in our companion paper^7^ provides the technical mechanism for enforcement.

### Agency disagreement: the quality information market has failed

The BeZero--Calyx anti-correlation of $\rho$ = -0.664 on the six-project REDD+ overlap sample is not merely a statistical curiosity. It means that two major carbon credit rating agencies, assessing the same projects using the same public information, produce rankings that are worse than random: an investor following BeZero's recommendations would systematically select the projects that Calyx ranks lowest, and vice versa. The mean inter-agency Spearman of +0.009 across all three agency pairs (BeZero--Calyx, BeZero--Sylvera, Calyx--Sylvera) is effectively indistinguishable from zero.

This finding is consistent with Carbon Market Watch's qualitative assessment^8^ that rating agencies "rate similar projects very differently," but our quantification is more severe than the qualitative description implies. An inter-agency correlation of +0.009 means that commercial ratings provide essentially no shared signal about relative project quality on the REDD+ projects where quality assessment matters most. The quality information market itself has failed: the institutions designed to resolve information asymmetry have not converged on a shared understanding of quality.

Two factors contribute to the disagreement. First, REDD+ credit quality depends heavily on counterfactual baselines --- what would have happened to the forest without the project --- and different agencies apply different assumptions about baseline deforestation rates, leakage, and permanence. Second, agencies weight quality dimensions differently: Calyx applies stringent cross-sectoral standards that penalise REDD+ projects relative to engineered removals, while BeZero treats additionality as a "limiting factor" rather than a strict cap.

Our framework's higher mean agreement with agencies ($\rho$ = +0.343 vs. +0.009) should not be interpreted as evidence that our framework is "more correct" than commercial agencies. Rather, it reflects a structural property of our design: by using explicit, documented weights and a transparent composite formula, the framework avoids the systematic divergences that arise when agencies apply implicit and potentially inconsistent methodological choices. On the expanded cross-methodology dataset ($n$ = 27), where quality signals are clearer (engineered CDR, industrial avoidance), our framework converges with BeZero at $\rho$ = +0.901, consistent with the observation that inter-agency disagreement is concentrated in credit categories where the underlying science is disputed.

### Open, reproducible quality infrastructure as prerequisite for market recovery

The combination of composition misattribution, complete quality collapse, and agency disagreement points to a single structural diagnosis: the VCM lacks transparent, reproducible quality infrastructure. Commercial ratings are proprietary and, on REDD+ projects, mutually contradictory. The CCP label is binary (pass/fail) and does not differentiate quality within the CCP-eligible universe (where PQD still varies from 0.076 to 0.419). No existing quality system provides a callable interface that smart contracts can use to enforce quality gates at the point of deposit or retirement.

Our framework addresses this gap through three design choices. First, openness: all rubrics are machine-readable JSON, the scoring engine is open-source, and the full dataset is published, enabling any researcher to substitute alternative weights and recompute every result in this paper. Second, continuous quality differentiation: six-tier grades (AAA through B) and the continuous PQD metric distinguish quality within and across the CCP-eligible universe, not merely at the CCP boundary. Third, composability: the on-chain implementation (ERC-CCQR standard, detailed in our companion paper^7^) exposes a `meetsGrade()` function callable by any smart contract at zero marginal gas cost, enabling quality gating to be embedded in pool design, retirement flows, and DeFi protocols without requiring trusted intermediaries.

### Limitations

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, the quality scores are author-derived: although validated against CCP labels ($d$ = 1.80), commercial agency ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901), and an independent LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.600, ICC = 0.993), the per-dimension rubric reflects a single research group's judgment informed by literature synthesis rather than by structured expert elicitation. The framework's architecture is designed for iterative calibration: we have identified 20 domain experts for a Best-Worst Scaling consultation to produce empirically grounded weight priors.

Second, the inter-rater reliability study uses three Claude-family language models (Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, Haiku 4.5) rather than human domain experts. All three models share training data and alignment procedures, which may produce correlated biases invisible to within-family comparisons. A multi-provider replication incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and an open-weight model is needed to establish that the rubric's reproducibility extends beyond a single model family.

Third, the rank correlation with commercial agencies uses $n$ = 6 for the REDD+-only dataset and $n$ = 27 for the expanded cross-methodology dataset (30 total projects, 12 types). While the expanded dataset substantially improves coverage relative to prior work, some methodology categories (ocean alkalinity enhancement, soil carbon) lack public agency ratings entirely.

Fourth, the BCT composition analysis relies on Verra registry metadata for project classification. Methodology categories are assigned by Verra at the project level; we did not independently verify the classification of each of the 168 projects. Misclassification at the registry level would propagate into our composition breakdown, though the dominance of renewable energy (69.1%) is large enough that even substantial reclassification would not change the qualitative finding.

Fifth, the PQD metric, like the composite score on which it is based, embeds normative assumptions: the Oxford Principles hierarchy (removal > avoidance), co-benefits excluded from the composite, and specific dimension weights. Users who disagree with these assumptions can re-weight dimensions and recompute all results using the published rubrics and scoring engine.

Sixth, the cross-pool comparison (BCT, NCT, CHAR) is observational and does not support causal inference. BCT and NCT share 88.6% of their underlying tokens and have overlapping depositor populations (19 wallets). Cluster-robust inference renders the naive BCT--NCT correlation difference non-significant ($p$ = 0.24 under token-clustered bootstrap vs. naive $p$ = 2.3 $\times$ 10$^{-7}$). NCT was designed after BCT's problems were observed, introducing design endogeneity. No independent control pool with temporal deposit data operated during BCT's 2021--2022 lifetime. Causal identification of pool-mechanism effects therefore remains an open question for future work, ideally exploiting natural variation in pool design across independent protocols.

Seventh, the temporal decline ($\rho$ = $-$0.24 on the full sample) is primarily driven by the vintage-year component of the composite score. A robustness check using a vintage-free composite reverses the sign ($\rho$ = +0.24), indicating that within the non-vintage quality dimensions, later deposits are not systematically worse. The temporal signal reflects vintage-composition drift (progressively older credits entering the pool), not within-vintage quality deterioration. We cannot distinguish whether the vintage drift reflects strategic dumping of debunked credits or supply exhaustion.

Eighth, the BeZero external validation covers only 7 BCT projects (1.8% of pool tonnage, $p$ = 0.073, not significant at $\alpha$ = 0.05). While the direction is consistent, the sample is too small for standalone inference.

## Abstract

The collapse of Toucan Protocol's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- from $7 to $0.50 --- is widely attributed to low-quality REDD+ credits flooding the market. We analyse all 1,187 on-chain deposit transactions (168 VCS projects, 22 million tonnes) on the Polygon blockchain and find this narrative is wrong. BCT was 69.1% renewable energy credits (grid-connected wind, hydro, and solar from China and India) and just 4.2% REDD+. This 69.1% represents a 1.87$\times$ over-selection relative to the Verra VCS registry base rate of approximately 37% renewable (binomial $p$ < 10$^{-154}$), confirming that the pool's composition reflects genuine selection, not passive market-supply reflection. Using an open, seven-dimension quality framework validated against the ICVCM Core Carbon Principles label (Cohen's $d$ = 1.80), BeZero Carbon ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901, $n$ = 27), and an independent rater panel (Fleiss' $\kappa$ = 0.600), we score all 345 unique tokens and compute the Pool Quality Deficit for BCT at 0.679, with 60.7% at grade B, 29.1% at BB, and 10.2% at BBB --- a low-type pooling equilibrium consistent with Gresham's law applied to uniform-price pools. Temporal quality degradation is present but modest ($\rho$ = $-$0.24 on the full sample, $p$ < 10$^{-12}$), with the signal already visible before the Terra/LUNA crash ($\rho$ = $-$0.12, $p$ = 0.007, $n$ = 491) and not attributable to KlimaDAO treasury activity (zero wallet overlap). A 34-segment quality atlas of the voluntary carbon market reveals 10-fold PQD variation (0.076 for direct air capture to 0.759 for grid-connected renewables). Quality-gated pools exhibit descriptively better outcomes --- BCT (no gate, PQD 0.679), NCT (nature-based filter, 0.601), CHAR (allowlist, 0.221) --- though these cross-pool comparisons are observational rather than causally identified, as no independent control pool operated during BCT's lifetime. Meanwhile, the quality information infrastructure itself is broken: BeZero and Calyx Global anti-correlate at $\rho$ = $-$0.664 on the same projects. These findings demonstrate that transparent, reproducible quality measurement is the prerequisite for carbon market recovery, and that the prevailing narrative about what destroyed the first major tokenized carbon market was factually wrong.
