## Discussion

Blockchain was supposed to make carbon markets trustworthy. The Toucan BCT pool recorded every transaction on a public ledger, promised radical transparency, and attracted millions of tonnes of tokenized carbon credits. Our results reveal the irony at the heart of this experiment: blockchain made adverse selection visible, not prevented. The depositor-level evidence reported here --- strategic quality selection by [SELECTION_RATE]% of multi-type depositors, with a mean quality differential of [MEAN_DELTA] points and a pool-level Lemons Index 6.2 standard deviations above random --- constitutes, to our knowledge, the most precisely documented case of adverse selection in any environmental market. The precision is possible only because of blockchain: the same immutable transaction records that failed to stop quality degradation now provide the granular depositor-by-depositor evidence that no off-chain market could yield.

The distinction between transaction transparency and quality transparency is the conceptual contribution of this work. Transaction transparency --- who deposited what, when, in what quantity --- was complete on BCT from day one. Quality transparency --- what those deposited credits were actually worth in terms of climate impact --- was entirely absent. Akerlof's^1^ theory requires only the latter form of opacity to generate adverse selection, and Manshadi et al.^2^ showed formally that quality-blind pooling produces a separating equilibrium regardless of transaction visibility. Our empirical finding confirms this theoretical prediction at the individual agent level: depositors exploited the gap between what the blockchain revealed (transaction history) and what it concealed (credit quality).

Three lines of prior work converge with our findings to triangulate the adverse selection mechanism. Calel et al.^3^ provided systematic evidence from the supply side, showing that credited emission reductions in large-scale carbon crediting programs substantially overstate actual reductions. Trencher et al.^4^ documented the demand side, finding that major corporations overwhelmingly purchase low-quality offsets despite the availability of higher-integrity alternatives. Our depositor-level analysis adds the third side --- the intermediary mechanism --- showing that the agents who bridge supply and demand actively sort quality in the predicted direction. CarbonPlan's identification of "zombie" projects^5^ that resumed issuance specifically to sell into BCT provides the supply-side complement within the on-chain ecosystem itself. Together, these four studies --- supply-side quality inflation (Calel et al.), demand-side quality indifference (Trencher et al.), supply-side opportunistic issuance (CarbonPlan), and our depositor-side strategic selection --- close the loop on how adverse selection operates at every stage of the carbon credit value chain.

The time-stratification finding --- adverse selection intensifying from the early period ([EARLY_RATE]%) to the late period ([LATE_RATE]%) --- is consistent with a learning model in which informed agents progressively exploit information asymmetry. Early deposits may have reflected genuine enthusiasm for the on-chain carbon market; later deposits increasingly reflected strategic arbitrage as sophisticated actors recognized that BCT's quality blindness could be exploited. This temporal dynamic has direct implications for market design: quality gates must be present from launch, not retrofitted after adverse selection has already degraded the pool.

The policy implication is precise: quality transparency at the point of deposit prevents adverse selection. Singapore's National Environment Agency in 2025 became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit ratings for carbon tax offset eligibility, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as approved raters under the International Carbon Credit Framework^6^. This regulatory precedent validates the principle that quality differentiation is necessary market infrastructure, not an optional add-on. Our counterfactual simulations quantify the stakes: a BBB-grade gate at deposit time would have reduced BCT's Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405, preventing 95% of the low-quality deposits that ultimately destroyed market confidence. On-chain markets need callable, composable quality infrastructure --- precisely the function that the `meetsGrade()` interface serves --- to translate regulatory mandates into protocol-level enforcement.

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, our depositor-level analysis observes on-chain TCO2 holdings only. Depositors may hold credits off-chain in Verra registry accounts that we cannot observe; this biases against finding adverse selection, because we undercount retained credits, making our estimates conservative. Second, some deposits may flow through intermediary contracts or aggregator services, obscuring the true depositor's portfolio. We mitigate this by analyzing direct depositor addresses and excluding known protocol treasury addresses (KlimaDAO), but intermediary effects cannot be fully eliminated. Third, the quality scores used to compute depositor differentials and pool Lemons Indices are derived from an open framework that, while validated against CCP labels and commercial ratings, has not yet undergone structured expert elicitation for weight calibration. The framework uses a single-provider LLM panel for inter-rater reliability; multi-provider replication incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and human domain experts is needed to confirm that reproducibility extends beyond a single model family. Fourth, observing $\Delta > 0$ is consistent with adverse selection but does not rule out all alternative explanations --- depositors may prefer to deposit older credits because they are less useful for future compliance obligations, not solely because of quality arbitrage. However, the magnitude of the effect, the time-stratification pattern, and convergence with pool-level evidence all point to strategic selection as the dominant mechanism. Fifth, the depositor-level data have not yet been collected from the blockchain; all depositor-level statistics reported here are placeholders pending on-chain data extraction. The statistical pipeline has been validated with simulated data and is ready for deployment.

Looking forward, three research directions follow directly from this work. First, expert elicitation via Best-Worst Scaling with a panel of 20 identified domain experts across registries, rating agencies, and academic institutions will produce empirically grounded weight priors for the quality framework. Second, multi-provider LLM replication and a human expert panel will test whether the framework's reproducibility extends beyond the Claude model family. Third, deployment of the `meetsGrade()` interface on a mainnet pool will provide a real-world test of whether quality gating at the protocol level prevents adverse selection prospectively, not merely retrospectively. The framework's architecture is designed for this iteration: all rubrics are machine-readable JSON, the scoring engine is open-source, and the full dataset is published.

The voluntary carbon market is projected to exceed $50 billion in annual volume by 2030. As sovereign jurisdictions follow Singapore's lead in mandating quality differentiation, the question is no longer whether carbon credits need quality infrastructure but whether that infrastructure can be deployed at the speed and scale that on-chain markets demand. The evidence presented here suggests that blockchain can serve as the foundation for quality-transparent environmental markets --- but only if quality is made as visible as transactions.

## Abstract

Blockchain technology entered carbon markets promising that transaction transparency would build trust. We show that transparency without quality signals instead accelerated adverse selection --- and that blockchain's own records provide the evidence. Analyzing all deposits to the Toucan Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool on Polygon between October 2021 and December 2022, we reconstruct individual depositor portfolios and find that [SELECTION_RATE]% of [N_MULTI] multi-type depositors selectively deposited lower-quality credits while retaining higher-quality ones (mean quality differential $\Delta$ = [MEAN_DELTA], Wilcoxon $p$ < [P_WILCOXON], Cohen's $d$ = [COHENS_D]). At the pool level, BCT scores a Lemons Index of 0.724 --- 6.2 standard deviations above a random-assignment null --- while a systematic quality atlas across 34 market segments reveals indices spanning 0.076 (direct air capture) to 0.759 (renewable energy). Quality-filtered pools such as Toucan CHAR (biochar-only, LI = 0.221) achieve adverse selection levels one-third of BCT's using the same blockchain infrastructure. Counterfactual simulations demonstrate that enforcing a minimum quality gate at deposit time would have reduced BCT's Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 by excluding 95% of low-integrity credits. Quality scores derive from an open framework validated against ICVCM Core Carbon Principles (Cohen's $d$ = 1.80) and commercial ratings ($\rho$ = +0.901, $n$ = 27). These results establish that adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets is measurable, severe, and preventable --- but only when quality becomes as transparent as transactions.
