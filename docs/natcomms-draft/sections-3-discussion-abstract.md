# Discussion and Abstract — Nature Communications Draft

**Paper**: "Quantifying adverse selection in tokenized carbon credit pools"

## Discussion

The Lemons Index provides, to our knowledge, the first quantitative measurement of adverse selection severity in tokenized carbon credit pools. Akerlof's 1970 theory predicts that markets for goods of heterogeneous, unobservable quality will be dominated by low-quality supply, but the voluntary carbon market has lacked a computable metric to diagnose this condition in practice. Our index translates the theory into a single number bounded between 0 (no adverse selection) and 1 (complete quality failure), computed directly from the composite quality scores of credits held in a pool. At BCT's historical peak, the Lemons Index of 0.724 means that the average tokenized credit scored below 28 out of 100 -- placing the pool mean firmly in the lowest grade band. This is consistent with independent evidence that fewer than 16% of carbon credits represent real emission reductions and that 87% of offsets purchased by major corporations carry high risk of providing no genuine climate benefit. The Lemons Index gives pool operators and regulators a monitoring signal analogous to how total value locked measures pool capitalization: a number that can be tracked over time, compared across pools, and used to trigger governance interventions when quality degrades beyond a threshold.

The approximately 0.5-point gap between BCT (0.724) and CHAR (0.221) quantifies what quality filtering is worth. CHAR achieves its low Lemons Index through a binary project allowlist restricted to biochar credits, a blunt instrument that cannot express continuous quality gradients. Our counterfactual simulations demonstrate that a graded quality gate -- implemented through the meetsGrade() smart contract interface -- can achieve comparable or superior results without restricting pool composition to a single project type. Applying a BBB minimum threshold to BCT's historical composition reduces its Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405, a 0.32-point improvement that admits only 5% of the original credits but eliminates the bulk of low-integrity supply (HFC-23 destruction, pre-2013 renewables, poorly verified REDD+). For Klima 2.0's more recent kVCM inventory, the same BBB gate reduces the Lemons Index from 0.519 to 0.273 while retaining 40% of credits. The meetsGrade() interface generalises binary curation to a continuous, parameterisable quality threshold that any pool or retirement contract can enforce at the protocol level. This finding has immediate policy relevance: Singapore's National Environment Agency in 2025 became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit ratings for carbon tax offset eligibility, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as approved raters under the International Carbon Credit Framework. The transition from voluntary to mandatory quality differentiation implies that on-chain markets will need callable, composable quality infrastructure -- precisely the function that meetsGrade() serves.

The framework's weight calibration rests on a combination of empirical validation and author judgment. The CCP calibration study shows a 1.99-grade gap between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits (Cohen's d = 1.80, CLES = 91.4%), matching the independently measured gap reported by Calyx Global, who found that CCP-eligible projects average an A rating versus C for non-CCP projects on their own scale. This convergence is achieved without training on CCP labels: the framework recovers the ICVCM's quality threshold from its own dimension weights. Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis with 10,000 Dirichlet-sampled weight perturbations (concentration = 50) yields 93.7% global grade robustness, indicating that the ordinal ranking of credits is stable across a wide range of plausible weight vectors. The five credits flagged as fragile (grade stability below 90%) are all boundary-adjacent cases within one grade step of a threshold, and their instability reflects genuine measurement uncertainty rather than structural dependence on any single weight. Nevertheless, the current weights derive from a single research group's judgment informed by literature synthesis rather than from structured expert elicitation. We have identified 20 domain experts across registries, rating agencies, and academic institutions for a Best-Worst Scaling consultation designed to produce empirically grounded weight priors. The framework's architecture is designed for this iteration: all rubrics are machine-readable JSON, the scoring engine is open-source, and the full pilot dataset is published -- enabling any researcher to substitute alternative weights and recompute every result in this paper.

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment. First, the inter-rater reliability study uses a panel of three Claude-family language models (Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, Haiku 4.5) rather than human domain experts. While the panel achieves substantial grade-level agreement (Fleiss' kappa = 0.600) and near-perfect composite reliability (ICC = 0.993), all three models share training data and alignment procedures, which may produce correlated biases invisible to within-family comparisons. A multi-provider replication incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and an open-weight model, followed by a human expert panel, is necessary to establish that the rubric's reproducibility extends beyond a single model family. Second, the rank correlation with commercial agencies, while strong on the combined REDD+ and cross-type dataset (rho = +0.913 versus BeZero, n = 15, permutation p < 0.001), rests on a modest sample; expansion to 30 or more credits with publicly documented agency ratings would narrow confidence intervals and test generalisability across underrepresented project types such as enhanced weathering and ocean alkalinity enhancement. Third, the framework rates credits on publicly available documentation -- project design documents, monitoring reports, and registry metadata -- rather than proprietary data held by registries or developers. This transparency is a design choice that enables reproducibility but may miss information available to commercial raters with registry partnerships. Fourth, the on-chain implementation is prototype-stage: the smart contracts are deployed on a testnet with a single-owner attestation model. The decentralised multi-rater architecture designed around the Ethereum Attestation Service requires broader ecosystem maturity -- multiple independent raters attesting to the same credits -- before it can replace centralised quality oracles. Fifth, additionality remains the weakest scoring dimension across all raters (Fleiss' kappa = 0.243), reflecting the fundamental difficulty of assessing counterfactual baselines from documentary evidence. This limitation is not unique to our framework; it is shared by every carbon quality assessment system and is the primary reason that commercial rating agencies disagree most sharply on projects where additionality is ambiguous.

Looking beyond carbon markets, the Lemons Index generalises to any pooled market for goods of heterogeneous, unobservable quality. Biodiversity credits, renewable energy certificates, plastic credits, and water quality offsets all face the same information asymmetry that drives adverse selection in carbon pools: buyers cannot distinguish high-integrity units from low-integrity units when they are commingled into fungible instruments. The meetsGrade() interface -- a view function that returns a boolean for any token against any quality threshold -- is a generic primitive that could serve as the quality-gating layer for these adjacent environmental markets as they move on-chain. More broadly, our results suggest that composable smart contract interfaces for quality attestation can prevent the race to the bottom that plagued early tokenized carbon markets. The counterfactual is instructive: had BCT enforced a meetsGrade(BBB) check at deposit time, it would have admitted only 2 of its 43 credits, but those 2 would have maintained a Lemons Index of 0.405 rather than 0.724 -- preserving buyer trust and potentially averting the market confidence collapse that followed. As voluntary carbon markets scale toward projected volumes exceeding $50 billion by 2030, and as sovereign jurisdictions follow Singapore's lead in mandating quality differentiation, transparent and composable quality measurement transitions from an academic exercise to essential market infrastructure. The framework presented here -- open-source, empirically validated, and deployable on-chain today -- offers a concrete foundation for that infrastructure.

## Abstract

Tokenized carbon credit pools aggregate credits of heterogeneous quality into fungible instruments, creating information asymmetry between informed sellers and uninformed buyers. Whether this adverse selection is quantitatively severe -- and whether market design can mitigate it -- has remained unmeasured. Here, we introduce the Lemons Index, a pool-level metric of adverse selection severity, and apply it to 318 credits spanning six on-chain pools. The Toucan Base Carbon Tonne pool scores a Lemons Index of 0.724, indicating that its average credit quality falls below 28 out of 100, while the quality-filtered CHAR pool scores 0.221. Our open, seven-dimension quality rating framework produces six-tier grades (AAA through B) validated by a 1.99-grade gap between credits eligible for the Integrity Council's Core Carbon Principles and those that are not (Cohen's d = 1.80), rank correlation of rho = +0.913 with BeZero Carbon ratings on 15 cross-type projects versus a mean inter-agency correlation of +0.009, and inter-rater reliability of Fleiss' kappa = 0.600 across an independent three-model panel. Counterfactual simulations show that enforcing a meetsGrade(BBB) quality gate on the BCT pool at deposit time would have reduced its Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 by admitting only 5% of credits. Implemented as a composable on-chain interface, this quality-gating mechanism generalises binary curation to continuous, parameterisable thresholds, providing the transparent quality infrastructure that tokenized environmental markets require to prevent adverse selection at scale.
