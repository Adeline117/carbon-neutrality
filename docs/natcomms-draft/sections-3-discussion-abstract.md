# Discussion

## 3. Discussion

The central irony of this paper is that blockchain technology made adverse selection *visible* without preventing it. The complete transparency of on-chain transaction records -- every deposit, every retention, every wallet balance -- is precisely what enabled us to reconstruct depositor-level portfolios and demonstrate that individuals systematically dumped low-quality credits into pools while keeping their better ones. No equivalent analysis is possible in the opaque, over-the-counter voluntary carbon market, where bilateral trades leave no public audit trail. Blockchain delivered on its promise of transparency; it failed on the implicit promise that transparency alone would discipline quality. The lesson for market design is that information must be *structured* -- attached to quality signals at the point of transaction -- not merely *available* on a public ledger.

### The measurement contribution

The quality framework presented here is, to our knowledge, the first open tool for carbon credit quality assessment that demonstrably exceeds the agreement level among commercial rating agencies. The framework-versus-BeZero correlation of *rho* = +0.901 (*n* = 27) compares with a mean inter-agency correlation of +0.009 -- a gap that reflects not our framework's superiority but the current state of commercial disagreement. BeZero and Calyx Global are anti-correlated at -0.664 on the same REDD+ projects, meaning that a buyer consulting both agencies would receive contradictory signals. The framework's value is not that it resolves this disagreement -- no single tool can, because the disagreement reflects genuine scientific uncertainty about counterfactual baselines -- but that it provides a transparent, reproducible, and re-weightable alternative whose assumptions are fully exposed rather than proprietary. The strongest independent validation is the BeZero rank correlation (*rho* = +0.901, *n* = 27), where the framework was developed without reference to BeZero's proprietary methodology. The CCP calibration (Cohen's *d* = 1.80, matching Calyx Global's independently reported gap of approximately two grade levels) provides convergent evidence under partially shared assumptions: the framework's weight allocation draws on the same integrity principles (additionality, permanence, MRV) that inform CCP assessment criteria, so the CCP separation represents partial convergent validation rather than fully independent external validation. We therefore position the BeZero correlation as the primary external validation and the CCP calibration as secondary convergent evidence.

### Completing the empirical triangle

Prior work has established two sides of the adverse selection triangle in carbon markets. Calel et al. demonstrated supply-side quality failure: fewer than 16% of credits represent real reductions, establishing that low-quality supply dominates the market. Trencher et al. demonstrated demand-side behaviour: 87% of corporate offset purchases carry high integrity risk, establishing that buyers do not effectively screen for quality. CarbonPlan's OffsetsDB documented supply-side quality degradation in on-chain pools specifically, showing that tokenized credits are dominated by projects with documented integrity concerns.

Our contribution closes the triangle with depositor-side evidence: the mechanism by which low-quality supply concentrates in pooled instruments. The depositor-level analysis shows not merely that BCT accumulated low-quality credits (which could reflect the composition of the tokenizable credit supply) but that depositors *chose* to deposit their lowest-quality credits while retaining their highest-quality ones. The distinction matters: the Pool Quality Deficit (PQD = 0.724) measures the outcome -- that BCT's quality is degraded -- while the Selection Index measures the mechanism -- that depositors strategically sort by quality. Supply-side quality failure (Calel), demand-side quality blindness (Trencher), supply-side on-chain degradation (CarbonPlan), and depositor-side strategic sorting (this study) together form a complete empirical account of how Akerlof's theoretical prediction manifests in a real market.

### The fix: quality information at the point of deposit

The counterfactual simulations demonstrate that quality gating at the point of deposit is sufficient to prevent the worst outcomes. A BBB-minimum gate would have reduced BCT's PQD from 0.724 to 0.405 by excluding 95% of credits. This is a dramatic intervention -- it implies that BCT in its historical form was not salvageable through marginal quality improvement -- but the CHAR pool's natural experiment shows that the resulting quality level is achievable and sustainable. CHAR's biochar-only allowlist achieves PQD = 0.221 without any sophisticated quality scoring, simply by restricting to a credit type that inherently scores well on the dimensions that matter most.

The policy implication is that quality gating is a market mechanism, not a technology solution. It can be implemented through regulatory mandate (as Singapore's NEA has begun to do with its International Carbon Credit Framework), through voluntary pool design (as CHAR demonstrates), or through protocol-level infrastructure (as the companion paper's on-chain implementation explores). The specific implementation matters less than the principle: credits entering a pooled instrument must carry quality information that is evaluable at the point of entry. Without this, the Akerlof mechanism activates and produces the outcomes we document.

### Limitations

Five limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment.

First, the quality scores are author-derived. While the CCP calibration and rank correlation analyses validate the framework at the population level, individual credit scores reflect the judgment of a single research group informed by public documentation rather than by proprietary data available to commercial raters. Expert elicitation through a structured Best-Worst Scaling consultation with 20 identified domain experts would strengthen the framework's weight calibration and address edge-case scoring disagreements.

Second, the inter-rater reliability study uses a panel of three Claude-family language models (Fleiss' *kappa* = 0.600, ICC = 0.993) rather than human domain experts. All three models share training data and alignment procedures, which may produce correlated biases invisible to within-family comparisons. A multi-provider replication incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and an open-weight model, followed by a human expert panel, is necessary to establish that the rubric's reproducibility extends beyond a single model family. The *kappa* of 0.600 sits at the boundary of Landis and Koch's "substantial" threshold; we report it without rounding up.

Third, the on-chain depositor analysis captures only credits visible on the Polygon blockchain. Off-chain holdings -- credits retained in Verra or Gold Standard registries without tokenization -- are invisible to our analysis. A depositor who tokenized only their lowest-quality credits (retaining high-quality credits off-chain) would be counted as having deposited their full portfolio, biasing the quality delta toward zero. Our estimates of adverse selection are therefore conservative: the true delta is likely larger than what on-chain data alone reveals.

Fourth, intermediary wallets complicate depositor identification. Some addresses in the BCT deposit record represent aggregators, market makers, or protocol contracts rather than the original credit holders. We mitigate this by filtering addresses with transaction patterns consistent with automated routing (see Methods), but some misclassification is inevitable. The robustness check excluding KlimaDAO's treasury addresses the largest single intermediary.

Fifth, the rank correlation with commercial agencies, while strong (*rho* = +0.901 with BeZero on *n* = 27), rests on a modest sample. Expansion to 50 or more credits with publicly documented agency ratings would narrow confidence intervals and test generalisability across underrepresented project types such as enhanced weathering and ocean alkalinity enhancement. The 95% bootstrap CI of [.783, .959] is informative but not tight.

### Future directions

Three extensions would strengthen the framework's empirical foundation. First, structured expert elicitation through Best-Worst Scaling would replace author-derived weights with empirically grounded priors, reducing dependence on any single research group's judgment. Second, multi-provider LLM replication would test whether the rubric's reproducibility generalises beyond the Claude model family. Third, deployment of the quality framework as a callable on-chain interface -- explored in detail in the companion paper -- would enable real-time quality gating on mainnet pool contracts, translating the measurement contribution into prevention infrastructure. As voluntary carbon markets scale toward projected volumes exceeding $50 billion by 2030, and as sovereign jurisdictions follow Singapore's lead in mandating quality differentiation, the transition from retrospective measurement to prospective prevention becomes the critical next step.
