**Paper**: "Adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets: who profited from the first pool collapse"

## Discussion

The informal narrative around BCT's collapse^16,17,18^ attributes it to REDD+ credits with contested baselines --- a plausible frame given the broader academic evidence of REDD+ overcrediting^2,4^ but one never verified against BCT's recorded composition. Our forensic reconstruction reveals a different picture: the pool was {{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% renewable energy credits --- CDM-era projects with near-zero additionality --- while a small number of type-specialist accounts extracted the credits that off-chain markets still valued. The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial, while the additionality failure of CDM-era renewables is structurally invisible: the wind farms exist, the electricity is real, but grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010.

Our contribution is distinct from recent related work. Manshadi et al.^5^ model adverse selection in the VCM theoretically; we provide the first credit-level empirical test, showing that BCT's lack of quality certification produced exactly the lemons dynamics their model formalises. Probst et al.^2^ and Calel et al.^46^ quantify overcrediting at the project level; we show what happens when those overcredited projects enter a pooled market --- how quality heterogeneity translates into participant-level extraction and \$4--8 million in realised profits.

A natural objection is that BCT's collapse reflects cryptocurrency pathologies rather than a structural carbon market problem. We argue the opposite. The mechanism we document is a variant of Akerlof's (1970) lemons problem: classical adverse selection assumes private information, but BCT demonstrates that uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets produces the same Gresham dynamic even when quality is fully observable --- a strictly stronger result, since it shows that transparency alone cannot prevent quality collapse without quality-differentiated pricing. This design-enabled adverse selection operates identically whether the ledger is a blockchain, a centralised exchange, or a paper registry. Three features distinguish this from a generic crypto collapse: (i) the underlying assets are real-world carbon credits with Verra registry entries; (ii) quality heterogeneity is determined by project-level additionality and permanence, not market sentiment; and (iii) the same Gresham dynamic operates in traditional carbon registries^29,43^. The policy implication --- that quality-differentiated pricing is necessary wherever heterogeneous carbon credits are pooled --- applies equally to registry-based markets and Article 6.4.

The 98.8% stranding rate for B-grade credits is the starkest outcome: nearly 10 million tonnes of nominally valid offsets are functionally worthless. We estimate the welfare gap at \$100--250 million (Monte Carlo median \${{welfare.welfare_gap_median_usd_millions}}M, 90% CI \$68M--\$252M). Paradoxically, BCT inadvertently removed 9.6 million tonnes of low-quality credits from active circulation --- a permanent illiquidity event no registry-level intervention has achieved. The pool's failure as a market may have been an inadvertent success as a quality filter.

BCT is a single pool, but three features make its mechanism generalizable. First, the underlying economic logic --- Gresham's Law under uniform pricing --- is not specific to blockchain or to carbon; it applies wherever heterogeneous assets are pooled at a single price, including registry-based VCM transactions where fungible credits of varying quality clear against undifferentiated compliance obligations. Second, the Article 6.4 mechanism under the Paris Agreement is designing exactly such pooled crediting systems at sovereign scale; BCT provides a fully observable test case of what happens when quality differentiation is absent^42,43^. Third, the tokenized real-world asset sector (\$10 billion and growing) is replicating the pool-based architecture across commodities, bonds, and receivables --- BCT is the first pool to complete a full quality-collapse cycle with auditable transaction data.

Independent cross-domain evidence from Curve Finance's stETH/ETH pool --- a tokenized asset market on Ethereum --- confirms the generality of the pattern. During the Terra/LUNA collapse, this pool shifted by 380,869 ETH-equivalent ($\sim$\$762M) toward the low-quality asset over three weeks, with composition shifts 40$\times$ the pre-crisis baseline. The BCT case is chronic; the Curve case is acute. Together they demonstrate that uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets produces selective extraction across different asset classes.

Recovery from a low-type equilibrium requires discontinuous intervention. A quality-volume frontier sweep reveals that even a minimal quality floor (composite $\geq$29) would have excluded the worst half of BCT's tonnage. Critically, gating must operate on both margins: BCT's dual-margin forensics show that exit-side extraction was at least as damaging as entry-side quality loading. Our findings provide direct empirical support for the ICVCM's Core Carbon Principles framework^8^: without quality differentiation, pooled markets will systematically allocate low-quality residuals to passive participants. The emerging ecosystem --- Singapore's carbon credit ratings mandate^15^, blockchain-based credibility indices^20^, and quality-gating protocols^10^ --- must operate on both entry and exit margins.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. The quality scores are author-derived, but core findings (composition, base-rate selection, type-level redemption differentials, within-token cross-pool comparison, compositional DiD) rely on transaction data and Verra credit-type classifications, not on our scoring framework. The framework is validated against CCP labels ($d$ = {{quality.ccp_cohens_d}}), commercial ratings ($\rho$ = +{{quality.bezero_rho}}, $n$ = 27), and an LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.6), with removal-type sensitivity and multi-provider replication checks (see Supplementary Methods). The cross-pool comparison is observational; causal identification of pool-mechanism effects remains open. The temporal decline ($\rho$ = $-$0.24) is a vintage-composition effect (vintage-free $\rho$ = +0.24). The bridge decomposition ({{bridge_decomposition.bct_coverage_pct}}% pass-through; 99.6% by tonnage) establishes that entry-side selection is primarily architectural rather than strategic --- the adverse selection operated through which credit types were attracted to tokenization in the first place (bridge-level), not through depositors cherry-picking within the bridged universe (pool-level). This is consistent with the design-enabled framing: the architecture created the conditions for quality collapse without requiring deliberate manipulation at any single stage.

## Abstract

Toucan's Base Carbon Tonne (BCT) pool --- the largest tokenized carbon credit pool (22 million tonnes) --- collapsed because of CDM-era renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality ({{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% of pool content), not REDD+ credits ({{composition.redd_pct_tonnes}}%) as widely claimed. Analysing all {{composition.n_deposits}} deposits, {{redemption.n_transfer_events}} redemptions, and 28,897 unique accounts, we reconstruct the first credit-level forensic accounting of a carbon market collapse. Renewables were selected at 1.87$\times$ the VCS base rate ($p$ < 0.0001, account-clustered). Two nearly disjoint populations (1.4% overlap) operated on opposite margins: depositors fed the pool indiscriminately, while type-specialist extractors removed credits that off-chain markets valued. Five accounts extracted 1.55 million tonnes; 9.6 million tonnes of low-quality renewables remain permanently stranded. Design-enabled adverse selection --- uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets despite observable quality differences --- produced this outcome without requiring private information. The same 14 nature-based tokens were 100% redeemed from BCT but only 28.5% from quality-gated NCT, confirming that pool design, not credit quality, determines asset fate. Quality-differentiated mechanism design is a precondition for carbon market integrity.
