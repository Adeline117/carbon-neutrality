**Paper**: "Adverse selection in tokenized carbon markets: who profited from the first pool collapse"

## Discussion

### The wrong credits, the wrong narrative

The informal narrative around BCT's collapse^16,17,18^ attributes it to REDD+ credits with contested baselines --- a plausible frame given the broader academic evidence of REDD+ overcrediting^2,4^ but one that was never verified against BCT's recorded composition. Our forensic reconstruction reveals a different picture: the pool was {{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% renewable energy credits --- CDM-era projects with near-zero additionality --- while a small number of type-specialist accounts extracted the credits that off-chain markets still valued. The misattribution likely arose because REDD+ quality problems are visible and controversial, while the additionality failure of CDM-era renewables is structurally invisible: the wind farms exist, the electricity is real, but grid-connected wind in China was already economically competitive by 2010. Quality infrastructure must address additionality with the same rigour currently applied to baseline accuracy.

Our contribution is distinct from recent related work. Manshadi et al.^5^ model adverse selection in the VCM theoretically, predicting that certification can mitigate lemons dynamics; we provide the first credit-level empirical test of that prediction, showing that BCT's lack of quality certification produced exactly the adverse selection their model formalises, while NCT's quality gate partially arrested it. Sievert et al.^17^ analyse KlimaDAO's token-level market structure and concentration but do not examine the underlying credit quality composition; our deposit-level reconstruction reveals that the concentration they observe reflects systematic quality-based extraction, not random portfolio behaviour. Probst et al.^2^ and Calel et al.^46^ quantify overcrediting at the project level; we show what happens when those overcredited projects enter a pooled market mechanism --- how the quality heterogeneity they document translates into participant-level extraction and $4--8 million in realised profits.

### A carbon market failure, not a cryptocurrency failure

A natural objection is that BCT's collapse reflects the pathologies of cryptocurrency speculation rather than a structural problem in carbon markets. We argue the opposite. The adverse selection mechanism we document --- uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets under voluntary participation --- is Akerlof's (1970) lemons problem applied to a specific market design, and operates identically whether the ledger is a blockchain, a centralised exchange, or a paper registry. The blockchain is the data source that makes forensic reconstruction possible (every deposit, redemption, and account is publicly recorded), not the cause of the outcome. Three features distinguish this from a generic crypto collapse: (i) the underlying assets are real-world carbon credits with Verra VCS registry entries, not speculative tokens; (ii) the quality heterogeneity that drives selection is determined by project-level additionality, permanence, and methodology --- attributes defined by the physical climate intervention, not by market sentiment; and (iii) the same Gresham dynamic operates in traditional carbon registries where fungible credits of heterogeneous quality are retired against undifferentiated compliance obligations^29,43^. BCT's transparency simply made the mechanism observable at credit-level resolution. The policy implication --- that quality-differentiated pricing or gating is necessary wherever heterogeneous carbon credits are pooled --- applies with equal force to registry-based voluntary markets and to the Article 6.4 mechanism under the Paris Agreement.

### 9.6 million tonnes stranded

The 98.8% stranding rate for B-grade credits is the starkest outcome: nearly 10 million tonnes of nominally valid carbon offsets are functionally worthless, already retired on Verra's registry but trapped in a pool no one will pay to redeem from. Using literature additionality rates^2,24^ and the social cost of carbon, we estimate the welfare gap at approximately \$100--250 million relative to a counterfactual market-average pool (Monte Carlo median \${{welfare.welfare_gap_median_usd_millions}}M, 90% CI \$68M--\$252M). This is an illustrative order-of-magnitude estimate conditional on both the additionality assumptions and the counterfactual composition.

A reasonable objection is whether BCT's outcome is worse than the counterfactual of no tokenization. Without BCT, these CDM-era renewable credits would have remained in Verra registries, occasionally purchased at \$0.50--1.00 by companies seeking inexpensive offsets to claim carbon neutrality. In that scenario, the credits circulate and generate false confidence in emission reductions that likely never occurred. BCT inadvertently removed 9.6 million tonnes of low-quality credits from active circulation --- a permanent illiquidity event that no registry-level intervention has achieved. The welfare cost of BCT is therefore not absolute but relative to a counterfactual where the same credits continued to be traded and retired against climate commitments. The pool's failure as a market may paradoxically have been a success as an inadvertent quality filter.

### Identification and limits

The 1.87$\times$ base-rate over-selection ($p$ < 0.0001, account-clustered) is the paper's strongest test, requiring no external control. The over-selection reflects a joint product of architectural incentives (uniform pricing under voluntary participation) and supply-side factors (holders of CDM-era renewables had large inventories and low opportunity costs); we characterise this as design-enabled rather than design-determined. The {{bridge_decomposition.bct_coverage_pct}}% bridge pass-through confirms that selection operated at the bridge level, not through pool-level cherry-picking.

The Granger causality ($n$ = 55 weeks, VAR(2) selected by AIC) is suggestive of lead-lag dynamics but underpowered and potentially overfitted (10 parameters on 55 observations). The first-differenced daily regression ($n$ = 330, $\beta$ = $-$1.8, $p$ < 0.001) tests a different hypothesis --- contemporaneous co-movement rather than temporal precedence --- but provides stronger evidence on a larger sample that quality and price are linked, and this feedback is BCT-specific ($R^2$ = 0.04 vs. ETH). The broader BCT--NCT comparison is observational (88.6% token overlap, cluster-robust $p$ = 0.24), but the within-token comparison of the 14 tokens deposited into both pools provides the strongest identification: the same credits were 100% redeemed from BCT versus 28.5% from NCT, with ARR tokens showing the most extreme contrast (100% vs. 0%). A deposit-level difference-in-differences panel (1,895 events) confirms the divergence: BCT quality fell 7.3 points more than NCT after the Terra collapse ($p$ = 0.004, cluster-robust; permutation $p$ < 0.0001; Bayesian $P$($\beta_3$ < 0) = 99.98%). The temporal decline ($\rho$ = $-$0.24) is a vintage-composition effect (vintage-free $\rho$ = +0.24). Account-level population separation (1.4% overlap) does not establish entity-level independence. The framing --- adverse selection under observable quality heterogeneity, driven by architectural pricing constraints --- shares deep structural similarities with Akerlof (1970) and Gresham's Law. The contribution is the empirical documentation of this pattern in a live tokenized market, not a claim of fundamental theoretical novelty.

### Cross-domain evidence

Curve Finance's stETH/ETH liquidity pool --- a tokenized asset market on a separate blockchain (Ethereum) --- provides independent cross-domain evidence. Curve's stableswap treats stETH (Lido's liquid staking derivative) and ETH as near-equivalent --- a uniform-pricing mechanism analogous to BCT's 1:1 credit tokenization. The Terra/LUNA collapse (May 7--12, 2022) triggered an exogenous quality shock as cascading liquidations (Celsius, Three Arrows Capital) raised stETH counterparty risk. Transaction-level data reveals a composition shift matching the adverse-selection prediction:

- **May 2**: stETH net +64,939 (low-quality flooding in), ETH net $-$93,270 (high-quality extracted). Composition shift: 158,209 ETH-equivalent --- **40$\times$ the pre-depeg baseline** of $\pm$4,000 ETH/week.
- **May 9** (Terra crash): Both assets drain, but ETH drains 74,087 more than stETH.
- **May 16**: stETH floods back 27$\times$ faster than ETH. Composition shift: 148,573.

Over three weeks, the pool shifted by 380,869 ETH-equivalent toward the low-quality asset (approximately \$762M at contemporaneous prices). The structural parallel to BCT is precise: near-uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets produced selective extraction of the high-quality component. Two disanalogies warrant note: Curve's stableswap is partially quality-actionable (effective prices deviate from 1:1 as composition shifts, unlike BCT's strict uniform pricing), and the acute episode is also consistent with a classical bank-run interpretation. The BCT case is chronic (gradual degradation under permissionless design); the Curve case is acute (exogenous shock). Together they demonstrate that the pattern is not specific to carbon markets, though the evidence base remains limited to two independent cases.

### Recovery requires quality infrastructure

Once a pool reaches a low-type equilibrium, recovery requires discontinuous intervention --- quality gating or quality-differentiated pricing --- not marginal improvement. A quality-volume frontier sweep across composite-score thresholds reveals the tradeoff: a BB gate ($\geq$30) reduces PQD from 0.683 (161 scored tokens) to 0.627 but retains only 37% of tonnage (78 tokens); a BBB gate ($\geq$45) achieves PQD 0.503 but retains only 8.7% (12 tokens). A sharp volume drop occurs near composite $\geq$29 (PQD 0.644, 47% volume), driven by the large cluster of CDM-era renewable tokens scoring between 28 and 29; even this minimal quality floor would have excluded the worst half of BCT's tonnage. This quality-volume tradeoff is the central design parameter for future pools: higher gates improve quality but reduce liquidity, and the optimal threshold depends on the intended use case (compliance retirement vs. speculative trading vs. long-term holding).

Critically, quality gating must operate on both margins. BCT's dual-margin forensics show that the exit side --- where concentrated, sophisticated actors conducted programmatic type-targeted extraction --- was at least as damaging as the entry side. A deposit-side gate alone would not prevent selective withdrawal of high-quality credits. Effective pool design must either apply quality-differentiated redemption pricing (charging a premium to redeem higher-quality credits, thereby discouraging selective extraction) or maintain net-composition quality floors that restrict redemptions pushing the pool below a threshold. Our findings provide direct empirical support for the ICVCM's Core Carbon Principles framework^8^: without quality differentiation, pooled carbon markets will systematically allocate low-quality residuals to passive participants while informed actors extract the high-quality fraction. The emerging ecosystem of quality infrastructure --- Singapore's 2025 mandate for carbon credit ratings^15^, blockchain-based credibility indices for carbon registries^20^, and the automated quality-gating protocol described in our companion paper^10^ --- represents steps in this direction, but must operate on both entry and exit margins to be effective.

### Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the quality scores are author-derived, and the paper's core findings are explicitly designed to not depend on them: BCT's composition (69.1% renewable), the 1.87$\times$ base-rate selection, the type-level redemption differentials, the within-token cross-pool comparison, and the compositional DiD ($t$ = 6.12, $p$ < 0.0001) all rely on on-chain data and Verra credit-type classifications, not on our scoring framework. The framework provides an additional analytical lens --- enabling PQD, quality-atlas, and quality-gate simulations --- and is validated against CCP labels ($d$ = {{quality.ccp_cohens_d}}), commercial ratings ($\rho$ = +{{quality.bezero_rho}}, $n$ = 27), and an LLM panel ($\kappa$ = 0.6), but the rubric reflects a single group's judgment. A potential circularity concern --- that the removal-type dimension (weight 0.250) mechanically reproduces the CCP label --- is refuted by a robustness check on the full 318-credit dataset: removing the removal-type dimension entirely and renormalizing weights preserves 101.4% of the CCP/non-CCP composite gap (27.7 vs. 27.3 points), with Cohen's $d$ increasing from 1.87 to 2.44 ($n$ = 318). The separation is driven by additionality, permanence, and MRV rather than by the removal-type dimension alone. The inter-rater study uses three Claude-family models that share training data, potentially producing correlated biases; a multi-provider replication (GPT-5, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Llama 4 Maverick, $n$ = 5) yields cross-provider $\kappa$ = 0.647, exceeding the Anthropic-only baseline, partially addressing this concern. The dimension weights embed normative assumptions (Oxford hierarchy, ICVCM CCP priorities); a pilot BWS expert panel ($n$ = 5) confirmed consistency with our initial weights ($\rho$ = +0.814, $\chi^2$ $p$ = 0.99) but is below the $n$ $\geq$ 20 target for stable estimation. A community-scale expert consensus exercise is planned; the framework architecture accepts updated weights without re-engineering, and all rubrics are published for independent re-weighting.

Second, the cross-pool comparison (BCT, NCT, CHAR) is observational. BCT and NCT share 88.6% of tokens and 19 depositor accounts; cluster-robust inference renders the naive correlation difference non-significant ($p$ = 0.24 vs. naive $p$ = 2.3 $\times$ 10$^{-7}$). No independent control pool operated during BCT's lifetime; causal identification of pool-mechanism effects remains an open question.

Third, the temporal decline ($\rho$ = -0.24) is driven by the vintage-year component of the composite. A vintage-free robustness check reverses the sign ($\rho$ = +0.24), indicating vintage-composition drift rather than within-vintage quality deterioration. We cannot distinguish strategic dumping from supply exhaustion.

Fourth, the BeZero BCT validation covers only 7 projects ($p$ = 0.073, not significant at $\alpha$ = 0.05). BCT composition relies on Verra registry metadata without independent verification, though the 69.1% renewable dominance is robust to misclassification.

Fifth, the bridge decomposition ({{bridge_decomposition.bct_coverage_pct}}% pass-through by token count; 99.6% by tonnage) establishes that entry-side selection is primarily architectural. The 24 non-BCT bridged tokens hold a combined 86,683 tonnes --- 0.4% of the 22.07 million total bridged tonnage --- confirming that pool-level selection space was negligible. We cannot fully decompose the 24 non-BCT tokens by credit type (14 of 24 lack on-chain name metadata). The tonnage pass-through is computed from `totalSupply()` on the non-BCT tokens (current supply after retirements), making the non-BCT figure a lower bound and the 99.6% pass-through an upper bound on bridge-level dominance.

## Abstract

The world's largest tokenized carbon credit pool collapsed because of renewable energy credits with near-zero additionality ({{composition.renewable_pct_tonnes}}% of pool content), not REDD+ credits ({{composition.redd_pct_tonnes}}%) as widely claimed. Analysing all {{composition.n_deposits}} deposits, {{redemption.n_transfer_events}} redemptions, and 28,897 unique accounts in Toucan's BCT pool (22 million tonnes of tokenized credits), we reconstruct the first complete forensic accounting of a tokenized carbon market collapse. Renewables were selected at 1.87$\times$ the VCS registry base rate ($p$ < 0.0001); 99.6% of all bridged tonnage entered BCT directly, indicating design-enabled rather than strategic selection. Two nearly disjoint account populations (1.4% overlap) operated on opposite margins: {{selection.n_wallets}} depositors fed the pool with what the bridge produced, while type-specialist extractors --- 15 of the 20 largest redeemers never deposited --- removed the credits off-chain markets still valued. Five accounts extracted 1.55 million tonnes of high-demand credits; 9.6 million tonnes of B-grade renewables remain permanently stranded in the pool. The pool's uniform pricing of quality-heterogeneous assets under voluntary participation was sufficient to produce this outcome despite full quality transparency. A within-token cross-pool comparison confirms that design drives outcome: the same 14 nature-based tokens were 100% redeemed from BCT but only 28.5% from quality-gated NCT. An independent tokenized asset market exhibits analogous compositional degradation during the same exogenous shock (380,869 ETH-equivalent shift, 40$\times$ baseline), suggesting the pattern generalises beyond carbon. Quality-actionable mechanism design is a precondition for tokenized market viability.
