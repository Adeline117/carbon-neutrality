# Discussion and Abstract — Nature Communications Draft

**Paper**: "Quantifying quality degradation in tokenized carbon credit pools"

---

## Discussion

The Lemons Index operationalises the measurement of quality degradation in tokenized carbon credit pools, providing a computable, pool-comparable metric grounded in an explicit null model. Akerlof's 1970 theory predicts that markets for goods of heterogeneous, unobservable quality will be dominated by low-quality supply, but the voluntary carbon market has lacked a quantitative metric to assess whether specific pools exhibit quality distributions consistent with this prediction. Our index translates pool composition into a single number bounded between 0 (no quality deficit) and 1 (complete quality failure), interpretable relative to a random-assignment baseline of 0.51. At BCT's historical peak, the Lemons Index of 0.724 --- 6.2 standard deviations above the null expectation --- means that the average tokenized credit scored below 28 out of 100, placing the pool mean firmly in the lowest grade band. This quality degradation is consistent with independent evidence that fewer than 16% of carbon credits represent real emission reductions and that 87% of offsets purchased by major corporations carry high risk of providing no genuine climate benefit. Importantly, the Lemons Index measures an outcome consistent with adverse selection --- disproportionate accumulation of low-quality credits --- rather than the mechanism itself. Whether this outcome reflects strategic depositor behaviour (informed sellers retaining better credits), quality-blind pooling rules that mechanically attracted low-quality supply, or both, cannot be determined from pool composition data alone. The Lemons Index gives pool operators and regulators a monitoring signal analogous to how total value locked measures pool capitalization: a number that can be tracked over time, compared across pools, and used to trigger governance interventions when quality degrades beyond a threshold.

The approximately 0.5-point gap between BCT (0.724) and CHAR (0.221) quantifies what quality filtering is worth. CHAR achieves its low Lemons Index through a binary project allowlist restricted to biochar credits, a blunt instrument that cannot express continuous quality gradients. Our counterfactual simulations model how a graded quality gate could achieve comparable or superior results without restricting pool composition to a single project type. In simulation, applying a BBB minimum threshold to BCT's historical composition reduces its Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405, a 0.32-point improvement that admits only 5% of the original credits but eliminates the bulk of low-integrity supply (HFC-23 destruction, pre-2013 renewables, poorly verified REDD+). For Klima 2.0's more recent kVCM inventory, the same BBB gate reduces the Lemons Index from 0.519 to 0.273 while retaining 40% of credits. Quality gating generalises binary curation to continuous, parameterisable thresholds --- a market design principle that can be implemented through smart contracts, registry rules, or regulatory mandates. This finding has immediate policy relevance: Singapore's National Environment Agency in 2025 became the first sovereign body to mandate commercial carbon credit ratings for carbon tax offset eligibility, appointing BeZero, Calyx Global, and Sylvera as approved raters under the International Carbon Credit Framework. The transition from voluntary to mandatory quality differentiation creates demand for transparent, reproducible quality assessment frameworks --- regardless of whether enforcement occurs on-chain or through traditional regulatory mechanisms.

The framework's weight calibration rests on a combination of empirical validation and author judgment. The CCP calibration study shows a 1.99-grade gap between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits (Cohen's d = 1.80, CLES = 91.4%), matching the independently measured gap reported by Calyx Global, who found that CCP-eligible projects average an A rating versus C for non-CCP projects on their own scale. This convergence is achieved without training on CCP labels: the framework recovers the ICVCM's quality threshold from its own dimension weights. Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis with 10,000 Dirichlet-sampled weight perturbations (concentration = 50) yields 93.7% global grade robustness, indicating that the ordinal ranking of credits is stable across a wide range of plausible weight vectors. The five credits flagged as fragile (grade stability below 90%) are all boundary-adjacent cases within one grade step of a threshold, and their instability reflects genuine measurement uncertainty rather than structural dependence on any single weight. Nevertheless, the current weights derive from a single research group's judgment informed by literature synthesis rather than from structured expert elicitation. We have identified 20 domain experts across registries, rating agencies, and academic institutions for a Best-Worst Scaling consultation designed to produce empirically grounded weight priors. The framework's architecture is designed for this iteration: all rubrics are machine-readable JSON, the scoring engine is open-source, and the full pilot dataset is published -- enabling any researcher to substitute alternative weights and recompute every result in this paper.

Several limitations warrant explicit acknowledgment.

First, and most fundamentally, the framework's empirical base rests on author-derived scores. All 318 credits were scored by the research team using publicly available project documentation, introducing the possibility that systematic biases in the authors' judgments propagate into both the quality scores and the Lemons Index values computed from them. We address this concern through three layers of external validation, each with its own limitations. The strongest external check is the CCP calibration: the framework recovers a 1.99-grade separation between CCP-eligible and non-CCP credits without training on CCP labels, converging independently with Calyx Global's published finding of an approximately two-grade gap on their proprietary scale. This convergence is notable because the CCP label was designed by an independent governance body (the ICVCM) using different dimension definitions and weighting criteria than our framework; the fact that both systems produce the same magnitude of separation provides evidence against systematic miscalibration. The rank correlation with BeZero (rho = +0.901 on 27 projects across 12 project types) provides partial external validation, though it is limited to a single commercial agency and to projects for which public ratings are available. The inter-rater reliability study (Fleiss' kappa = 0.600, ICC = 0.993) demonstrates scoring reproducibility but uses a panel of Claude-family language models rather than human domain experts. All three models share training data and alignment procedures, which may produce correlated biases invisible to within-family comparisons. Full validation of the scoring framework requires human expert panels and multi-provider LLM replication (incorporating GPT-5, Gemini, and open-weight models), both of which are planned but not yet completed.

Second, the Lemons Index measures pool-level quality degradation --- an outcome consistent with adverse selection --- but does not establish that depositors acted strategically. Confirming the mechanism of adverse selection, as distinct from its outcome, would require depositor-level analysis: tracking whether the same entities that deposited low-quality credits into pools simultaneously held higher-quality credits off-chain or in wallets. If depositors held uniformly low-quality portfolios and deposited indiscriminately, the observed quality degradation would reflect quality-blind pooling rather than strategic selection. On-chain transaction data and registry retirement records could in principle support such an analysis, but the pseudonymous nature of blockchain addresses and the difficulty of linking on-chain wallets to off-chain registry accounts make this a substantial empirical challenge. Until depositor-level evidence is available, our results should be interpreted as documenting quality distributions consistent with adverse selection predictions, not as proof that adverse selection occurred.

Third, the rank correlation with commercial agencies, while strong on the expanded cross-type dataset, is based on a limited overlap sample. Further expansion to include underrepresented project types such as ocean alkalinity enhancement would test the limits of generalisability.

Fourth, the framework rates credits on publicly available documentation --- project design documents, monitoring reports, and registry metadata --- rather than proprietary data held by registries or developers. This transparency is a design choice that enables reproducibility but may miss information available to commercial raters with registry partnerships.

Fifth, additionality remains the weakest scoring dimension across all raters (Fleiss' kappa = 0.243), reflecting the fundamental difficulty of assessing counterfactual baselines from documentary evidence. This limitation is not unique to our framework; it is shared by every carbon quality assessment system and is the primary reason that commercial rating agencies disagree most sharply on projects where additionality is ambiguous.

Looking beyond carbon markets, the Lemons Index generalises to any pooled market for goods of heterogeneous, unobservable quality. Biodiversity credits, renewable energy certificates, plastic credits, and water quality offsets all face the same information asymmetry that can drive quality degradation in pooled instruments: buyers cannot distinguish high-integrity units from low-integrity units when they are commingled into fungible tokens. The quality gating principle demonstrated here --- filtering pool admission based on graded quality thresholds --- is applicable regardless of whether enforcement occurs through smart contracts, registry rules, or regulatory mandates. The counterfactual is instructive: had BCT enforced a BBB minimum threshold at deposit time, it would have admitted only 2 of its 43 credits, but those 2 would have maintained a Lemons Index of 0.405 rather than 0.724 --- preserving buyer trust and potentially averting the market confidence collapse that followed. As voluntary carbon markets scale toward projected volumes exceeding $50 billion by 2030, and as sovereign jurisdictions follow Singapore's lead in mandating quality differentiation, transparent and reproducible quality measurement transitions from an academic exercise to essential market infrastructure. The framework presented here --- open-source, empirically validated, and available as both a Python scoring engine and a reference Solidity implementation --- offers a concrete foundation for that infrastructure.

## Abstract

Tokenized carbon credit pools aggregate credits of heterogeneous quality into fungible instruments, creating conditions for quality degradation consistent with adverse selection theory. Whether the resulting quality distributions are quantitatively severe --- and whether market design can mitigate them --- has remained unmeasured. Here, we present an open quality assessment framework and apply it to measure quality distributions in six on-chain carbon credit pools spanning 318 credits. We operationalise pool-level quality measurement through the Lemons Index, interpretable relative to a random-assignment null model. The Toucan Base Carbon Tonne pool scores a Lemons Index of 0.724 (6.2 standard deviations above the null expectation), indicating that its average credit quality falls below 28 out of 100, while the quality-filtered CHAR pool scores 0.221. Our seven-dimension framework produces six-tier grades (AAA through B) validated by a 1.99-grade gap between credits eligible for the Integrity Council's Core Carbon Principles and those that are not (Cohen's d = 1.80), rank correlation of rho = +0.901 with BeZero Carbon ratings on 27 projects spanning 12 project types, and inter-rater reliability of Fleiss' kappa = 0.600 across an independent three-model panel. Counterfactual simulations show that enforcing a BBB quality gate on the BCT pool would have reduced its Lemons Index from 0.724 to 0.405 by admitting only 5% of credits. Quality gating generalises binary curation to continuous, parameterisable thresholds, providing transparent quality infrastructure for tokenized environmental markets.
